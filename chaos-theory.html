Taylor Series: Approximating the Complex with the Simple

#24 June 15, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

How does your calculator compute sin(0.3) or e^2.5? These functions aren't directly computable—they're defined by their mathematical properties. The answer involves Taylor series: representing any sufficiently smooth function as an infinite sum of polynomial terms. Polynomials are easy to compute—just multiplications and additions. Taylor series convert complex functions into polynomial approximations accurate to any desired precision.

The Formula

A Taylor series expands a function f(x) around a point a as:

f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) + f''(a)(x−a)²/2! + f'''(a)(x−a)³/3! + …

Each term involves the nth derivative of f evaluated at a, divided by n!. The zeroth-order approximation is just f(a). Adding the first derivative term gives a line. Adding the second gives a parabola. More terms yield increasingly accurate approximations.

Famous Examples

Around x = 0 (Maclaurin series):

e^x = 1 + x + x²/2! + x³/3! + … (converges everywhere)
sin(x) = x − x³/3! + x⁵/5! − … (odd terms, alternating)
cos(x) = 1 − x²/2! + x⁴/4! − … (even terms, alternating)

Euler's Formula

Taylor series reveal one of mathematics' most stunning identities. Substituting ix into the exponential series and separating real and imaginary parts:

e^(ix) = cos(x) + i·sin(x)

Setting x = π: e^(iπ) + 1 = 0, combining e, i, π, 1, and 0 in a single equation. Taylor series make this connection transparent—they reveal that exponentials and trigonometric functions are the same function, evaluated at different arguments.

Physics Applications

Taylor series are indispensable in physics. For small angles, sin(θ) ≈ θ—the first Taylor term—simplifying pendulum analysis. Special relativity's energy expands to recover Newtonian kinetic energy as the leading correction:

E ≈ mc² + ½mv² + higher order terms

Quantum mechanics uses perturbation theory—essentially a Taylor expansion in the strength of a small interaction—to solve problems that can't be solved exactly.

Conclusion

Taylor series reveal that smooth functions are, in a precise sense, made of polynomials. By expanding in derivatives, they expose a function's local structure and enable efficient computation. From calculator chips to quantum physics, Taylor series provide the bridge between abstract mathematical functions and concrete numerical computations—turning the complex into the polynomial, one derivative at a time.
