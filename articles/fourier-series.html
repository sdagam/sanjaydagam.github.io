Fourier Series: Representing Functions as Infinite Sums

#89 of 156 September 13, 2025 Group 4 · Jul–Dec 2025

When you talk into a phone, your voice gets broken down into hundreds of pure tones, compressed, transmitted digitally, and then reassembled at the other end — all in a fraction of a second. The mathematical principle that makes this possible was discovered by a French mathematician in the early 1800s while trying to understand how heat flows through metal. His name was Joseph Fourier, and his insight changed every field from telecommunications to medicine.

The Surprising Claim

Fourier's insight: almost any repeating signal — no matter how complex — can be built by adding together simple sine waves at different frequencies. Your voice is complicated: it rises, falls, has sharp consonants and smooth vowels. But underneath, it's a sum of pure tones. Add enough of them at the right frequencies and amplitudes, and you reconstruct any sound exactly.

This seems hard to believe. A square wave — something that jumps instantly between high and low — looks nothing like a smooth sine wave. Yet you can build it from sines:

Square wave = sin(x) + sin(3x)/3 + sin(5x)/5 + sin(7x)/7 + ... Add more terms → sharper corners → perfect square wave in the limit

Each term adds a higher frequency component that sharpens the corners. With infinitely many terms, you get the exact square wave. With just a few, you get a good approximation.

How You Find the Ingredients

Given a signal, how do you find how much of each frequency is in it? By integration. For a signal f(x) that repeats with period 2π, the amount of the frequency-n component is found by multiplying the signal by that frequency's sine or cosine and integrating over one period:

Amount of cos(nx) in f: aₙ = (1/π) ∫ f(x)·cos(nx) dx Amount of sin(nx) in f: bₙ = (1/π) ∫ f(x)·sin(nx) dx

The integral acts like a filter — it extracts just the component you're looking for and ignores the others. This works because different frequencies are orthogonal (their integrals against each other equal zero), so each frequency can be extracted independently without interference from the others.

Voice Compression on Your Phone

When you speak into a phone, software computes the Fourier coefficients (aₙ and bₙ) of short chunks of your audio. Human speech contains frequencies mostly between 300 Hz and 3400 Hz — outside that range, there's almost nothing. So the phone transmits only the coefficients for those frequencies and throws the rest away. That's why phone audio sounds slightly tinny compared to in-person speech: the very high and very low frequencies have been discarded.

MP3 compression works the same way, just more aggressively: frequencies that the human ear is less sensitive to are discarded or reduced. The result is a file one-tenth the size of the original recording that sounds nearly identical to most listeners.

Other Applications

MRI machines use Fourier analysis to reconstruct body images from radio frequency measurements. Seismologists decompose earthquake signals into frequency components to identify the depth and type of the quake. Astronomers search for periodic signals in telescope data by looking for spikes in the Fourier spectrum. Anywhere a signal repeats, Fourier series finds its hidden frequency fingerprint.

Conclusion

Fourier's discovery — that any repeating signal is a sum of sines and cosines — turned out to be one of the most practical insights in mathematical history. It's how your phone compresses voice, how doctors image your brain, how meteorologists analyze climate cycles, and how engineers test the vibration tolerance of bridges. The math is calculus: integration finds each frequency's contribution. The payoff is a complete recipe for reconstructing complexity from simplicity.
