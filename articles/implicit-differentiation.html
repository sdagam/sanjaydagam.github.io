Implicit Differentiation: Finding Slopes Without Solving for y

#56 of 156 January 25, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

The equation x² + y² = 25 defines a circle. To find the slope of the tangent line at any point, you could solve for y = ±√(25 − x²) and differentiate—but that requires choosing a branch and is algebraically messy. Implicit differentiation offers a cleaner approach: differentiate both sides of the original equation with respect to x, treating y as a function of x, and solve for dy/dx. This technique handles curves that can't be expressed as explicit functions, and it reveals relationships between rates of change without ever isolating one variable.

The Method

The key rule: when differentiating a term involving y with respect to x, apply the chain rule and multiply by dy/dx. For x² + y² = 25: differentiating both sides gives 2x + 2y(dy/dx) = 0, so dy/dx = −x/y. At the point (3, 4), the slope is −3/4. No square roots, no branch selection—the answer appears directly. The implicit equation encodes the relationship between x and y; differentiating implicitly extracts the rate of change relationship without needing to solve for y explicitly.

x² + y² = 25 → 2x + 2y·(dy/dx) = 0 → dy/dx = −x/y

Higher Derivatives

Implicit differentiation extends to higher-order derivatives. For d²y/dx², differentiate dy/dx = −x/y implicitly again: d²y/dx² = −[y − x(dy/dx)] / y². Substituting dy/dx = −x/y: d²y/dx² = −[y − x(−x/y)] / y² = −(y² + x²)/y³. For the unit circle (x² + y² = 1), this gives d²y/dx² = −1/y³—the curvature relationship. This process, though algebraically involved, is systematic: apply the quotient or product rule to the first derivative expression, treating dy/dx as a function, and substitute back using the original equation to simplify.

Curves That Can't Be Solved Explicitly

Many important curves resist explicit solution. The folium of Descartes: x³ + y³ = 3xy. Differentiating implicitly: 3x² + 3y²(dy/dx) = 3y + 3x(dy/dx), giving dy/dx = (y − x²)/(y² − x). This slope formula works anywhere on the curve without solving the cubic for y. The lemniscate of Bernoulli, the Cassini ovals, and countless curves in algebraic geometry are most naturally studied through implicit differentiation. Even in three dimensions, implicit surfaces F(x,y,z) = 0 are differentiated this way to find tangent planes and normal vectors.

Folium of Descartes: x³ + y³ = 3xy → dy/dx = (y − x²)/(y² − x)

Inverse Functions

Implicit differentiation elegantly derives derivatives of inverse functions. To find d/dx[arcsin(x)]: let y = arcsin(x), so sin(y) = x. Differentiating implicitly: cos(y)(dy/dx) = 1, giving dy/dx = 1/cos(y). Since sin(y) = x, cos(y) = √(1 − x²), so dy/dx = 1/√(1 − x²)—the standard result. The same technique gives derivatives of arccos, arctan, and all inverse hyperbolic functions without requiring direct inversion. This approach reveals why inverse function differentiation always involves the original function: implicit differentiation inherently connects the derivative of a function to the derivative of its inverse.

y = arcsin(x) → sin(y) = x → cos(y)·(dy/dx) = 1 → dy/dx = 1/√(1−x²)

Applications in Related Rates

Implicit differentiation is the engine behind related rates problems—the same technique applied to equations relating multiple quantities that all change with time. When you differentiate x² + y² = r² with respect to time t, you get 2x(dx/dt) + 2y(dy/dt) = 2r(dr/dt), relating the rates at which x, y, and r change simultaneously. This implicit approach avoids the need to isolate any variable and naturally produces equations connecting multiple rates of change, making it the standard tool for analyzing how related physical quantities evolve together.

Conclusion

Implicit differentiation extends calculus beyond explicitly defined functions to the broader world of implicitly defined curves, surfaces, and relationships. By treating the implicit equation as a constraint and differentiating through it using the chain rule, slopes and rates emerge naturally without algebraic untangling. This technique—simple in concept but powerful in application—handles curves that defy explicit expression and provides elegant derivations of results from inverse functions to surface normals that would be far more cumbersome by any other method.
