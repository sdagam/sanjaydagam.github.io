Bayes' Theorem: Updating Beliefs with Evidence

#6 February 10, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

A medical test is 99% accurate, and you test positive for a rare disease affecting 1 in 10,000 people. What's the probability you actually have the disease? Most people—including many doctors—guess around 99%. The actual answer is less than 1%. Understanding why requires Bayes' Theorem, perhaps the most important formula in probability.

The Formula

Bayes' Theorem relates conditional probabilities:

P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B)

In words: the probability of hypothesis A given evidence B equals the probability of observing B if A is true, times the prior probability of A, divided by the total probability of observing B. P(A) is the prior, P(B|A) is the likelihood, and P(A|B) is the posterior.

Solving the Medical Test Puzzle

Let A = "having the disease" and B = "testing positive." Prior: P(A) = 0.0001. Sensitivity: P(B|A) = 0.99. False positive rate: P(B|not A) = 0.01.

P(B) = 0.99 × 0.0001 + 0.01 × 0.9999 = 0.010098
P(A|B) = 0.99 × 0.0001 / 0.010098 ≈ 0.0098 (less than 1%)

The rare prior probability overwhelms the test's accuracy. Among every 10,000 people tested, about 100 will be false positives but only 1 will be a true positive.

Why This Matters

The medical test example illustrates a crucial lesson: evidence must be interpreted in context. The same test result means very different things depending on the base rate. For a common condition, a 99% accurate test is highly informative. For a rare condition, it produces mostly false alarms.

Applications Beyond Medicine

Bayes' Theorem underlies spam filtering—words like "lottery" increase spam probability because they appear more often in spam than legitimate email. Courtroom evidence should be interpreted Bayesianly: DNA match probabilities must be combined with prior probability of guilt given other evidence. Search and rescue operations use Bayesian updating to prioritize search areas.

Conclusion

Bayes' Theorem corrects our intuition about conditional probability. It reminds us that context—the prior probability—matters enormously when interpreting evidence. In a world flooded with data, Bayesian reasoning helps us update beliefs appropriately, avoiding both under-reaction and overreaction to new information.
