Vectors and Matrices: The Language of Linear Algebra

#79 of 156 July 5, 2025 Group 4 · Jul–Dec 2025

Every time your phone's GPS tells you to turn left in 200 feet, it's doing arithmetic on lists of numbers. Not single numbers — whole collections of them, describing your position, your speed, and the shape of the road all at once. Those collections are called vectors, and the rules for working with them are the foundation of linear algebra.

What's a Vector, Anyway?

A vector is just an ordered list of numbers. Your GPS position might be (43.05, -76.14) — latitude and longitude. A weather station might record (temperature, humidity, wind speed) as a single package: (72, 65, 12). Each number is a component, and together they describe something more complete than any single number could.

What makes vectors powerful is that you can do arithmetic with them. Add two vectors by adding their components. Multiply a vector by a number (called a scalar) by multiplying every component. So if your position is (43.05, -76.14) and you move (0.01, 0.02) degrees, your new position is simply (43.06, -76.12). The math is just component-by-component addition.

Enter the Matrix

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers. Think of it as a spreadsheet. A 3×2 matrix has 3 rows and 2 columns. But a matrix isn't just a way to store numbers — it's a set of instructions. When you multiply a matrix by a vector, you get a new vector. The matrix transforms the input into an output.

A = [[2, 0], v = [3, → Av = [6, [0, 3]] 2] 6]

This matrix doubled the first component and tripled the second. It stretched the vector. That's what matrices do — they rotate, stretch, squish, or flip whatever vector they act on. Every transformation a GPS app uses to convert your real-world coordinates into pixels on a map is a matrix multiplication happening behind the scenes.

Why This Matters for GPS

Your GPS chip receives signals from satellites. Each satellite's signal is a vector of timing and frequency data. The software stacks those signals into a matrix and solves a matrix equation to find your exact position. The same type of calculation — multiply a matrix by a vector, solve for an unknown vector — shows up in the map rendering, the route calculation, and the turn-by-turn display. Linear algebra is the engine running under the hood.

The Same Math, Many Problems

Once you see vectors and matrices, you notice them everywhere. A photo is a matrix of pixel values. Netflix's recommendation engine is built on matrices of ratings. A structural engineer describes forces on a bridge as vectors and uses matrix equations to find stress points. The rules are always the same: add vectors component by component, multiply matrices by following a specific dot-product procedure, and solve matrix equations to find unknown quantities.

Conclusion

Vectors and matrices are the vocabulary of modern applied mathematics. They let us handle many numbers at once, describe transformations precisely, and solve huge systems of equations efficiently. If calculus taught you how to handle change in one variable, linear algebra teaches you how to handle change in many variables simultaneously. That's why it underpins GPS navigation, image processing, machine learning, and almost every field of quantitative science.
