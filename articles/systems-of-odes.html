Systems of Differential Equations: Coupled Dynamics

#118 of 156 April 4, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Wolves eat rabbits. Rabbits eat grass. When wolves are numerous, rabbit populations crash. When rabbits are scarce, wolves starve and their population falls. With fewer wolves, rabbits recover — and the cycle repeats. This predator-prey dance has been observed in real ecosystems for centuries, and it follows a remarkably clean mathematical pattern: two populations, each changing at a rate that depends on both. This is a system of differential equations, and the mathematics that describes wolves and rabbits also describes competing businesses, interacting chemical reactions, and the electrical signals in your heart.

One Equation Isn't Enough

Article #106 introduced differential equations where one quantity changes over time. But most real systems involve multiple quantities that influence each other simultaneously. The rabbit population changes based on both the current number of rabbits (they breed) and the current number of wolves (they get eaten). The wolf population changes based on both the current number of wolves (they die without food) and the current number of rabbits (their food source). You need two equations that run simultaneously.

Lotka-Volterra predator-prey equations: dR/dt = aR - bRW (R = rabbits, W = wolves) dW/dt = cRW - dW dR/dt = rate of change of rabbit population aR = rabbits reproduce at rate a (more rabbits → more baby rabbits) bRW = rabbits get eaten at rate proportional to both populations cRW = wolves gain from eating rabbits (same encounters, different outcome) dW = wolves die at rate d without enough food

The term RW appears in both equations — it captures the encounters between the two species. Those same encounters reduce rabbits (bRW term) and increase wolves (cRW term). The system is coupled: you can't solve one equation without knowing the other.

The Oscillating Solution

Start with many rabbits and few wolves. Rabbits multiply freely (few predators). Wolves thrive on the abundant food — wolf population rises. As wolves increase, they eat rabbits faster than rabbits reproduce — rabbit population falls. With few rabbits, wolves starve — wolf population falls. With few wolves, rabbits recover — and the cycle begins again. Both populations oscillate, but out of phase: rabbit peaks precede wolf peaks, and wolf troughs follow rabbit troughs.

This oscillation has been observed in real data. Hudson's Bay Company kept fur-trapping records from 1845 to 1935 showing hare and lynx populations oscillating with roughly a 10-year period — a remarkable confirmation that nature follows mathematics.

Linear Systems: The Easier Case

When the equations are linear — each rate of change is a constant times the current values, with no products like RW — the system can be written as a matrix equation and solved exactly using eigenvalues.

Linear system: dx/dt = Ax x = vector of all quantities (e.g., [rabbit population, wolf population]) A = matrix of interaction coefficients Solution: x(t) = c₁e^(λ₁t)v₁ + c₂e^(λ₂t)v₂ λ₁, λ₂ = eigenvalues of A v₁, v₂ = eigenvectors of A c₁, c₂ = constants set by initial conditions Each eigenvalue controls the growth rate of one "mode" of the system.

If both eigenvalues are negative, both modes decay — the system returns to equilibrium (stable). If one eigenvalue is positive, that mode grows — the system is unstable. If the eigenvalues are complex numbers (a + bi), the solution oscillates — the real part determines whether it grows or decays, and the imaginary part determines the oscillation frequency. Complex eigenvalues are the mathematical signature of cyclical behavior.

Other Applications

Systems of differential equations model competing species in ecology, competing companies sharing a market, chemical reactions where multiple molecules interact simultaneously, the SIR model of epidemic spread (Susceptible, Infected, Recovered — three coupled equations), and the electrical activity of neurons where sodium and potassium currents interact to produce the firing of action potentials. The Hodgkin-Huxley model, which won the 1963 Nobel Prize in Physiology, is a system of four coupled differential equations describing how nerve impulses propagate.

Conclusion

Systems of differential equations model the real world's interacting parts. When wolves eat rabbits, two populations couple their rates of change, producing oscillations that match a century of real ecological data. Linear systems can be solved exactly using eigenvalues — the eigenvalues' signs and imaginary parts reveal whether the system stabilizes, explodes, or oscillates. From ecology to epidemics to neuroscience, coupled differential equations are the mathematical language of systems that influence each other over time.
