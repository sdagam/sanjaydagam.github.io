Tensor Decompositions: Extending Matrices to Higher Dimensions

#123 of 156 May 9, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Netflix doesn't just know that you like action movies and comedies — it knows that you like action movies on Friday nights and comedies on Sunday mornings, and that your preferences shift depending on whether you're watching alone or with family. A simple rating matrix (users × movies) can't capture this three-way interaction of user, movie, and context. What you need is a three-dimensional array — a tensor — and the mathematics to analyze it. Tensor decompositions are how data scientists extract structure from data with more than two dimensions.

From Matrices to Tensors

A matrix is a two-dimensional array: rows and columns. A tensor is the generalization to any number of dimensions. A 3D tensor (a "cube" of numbers) has three indices: the entry at position (i, j, k) might represent the rating of user i for movie j in context k (time of day, device, viewing company). A 4D tensor adds another dimension. Tensors are how data scientists naturally represent datasets with multiple interacting factors.

Color images are 3D tensors: height × width × color channel (red, green, blue). Videos are 4D tensors: height × width × color × time. Brain activity measured by fMRI is a 4D tensor: three spatial dimensions plus time. Any structured data with more than two interacting dimensions benefits from tensor methods.

The CP Decomposition: Rank-1 Terms

The most natural tensor decomposition is the CP (CANDECOMP/PARAFAC) decomposition. It expresses a tensor as a sum of simple "rank-1" tensors — each one the outer product of several vectors, one per dimension.

CP Decomposition of a 3D tensor X: X ≈ Σᵣ aᵣ ⊗ bᵣ ⊗ cᵣ (sum of R rank-1 terms) aᵣ = vector describing pattern r in dimension 1 (e.g., user types) bᵣ = vector describing pattern r in dimension 2 (e.g., movie genres) cᵣ = vector describing pattern r in dimension 3 (e.g., contexts) ⊗ = outer product (creates a 3D block from three vectors) In plain English: each term captures one "latent pattern" — e.g., "action-movie-loving users who watch late at night on phones." The full tensor is a sum of these pure patterns.

Each rank-1 term is interpretable: it represents a coherent pattern across all three dimensions simultaneously. In the Netflix example, one term might capture "early-adopter users, sci-fi movies, weekend mornings on smart TVs." The decomposition finds these patterns automatically from the data, without being told what to look for.

Tucker Decomposition: A Core and Factor Matrices

The Tucker decomposition is more flexible: X ≈ G ×₁ A ×₂ B ×₃ C, where G is a smaller "core tensor" capturing interactions between modes, and A, B, C are factor matrices compressing each dimension. Tucker is like SVD generalized to 3D — it finds the best low-dimensional approximation along each dimension simultaneously while capturing cross-mode interactions in G.

Unique Identifiability: Tensors Beat Matrices

One remarkable property: for matrices, the low-rank decomposition is not unique — many different pairs of matrices give the same product. For tensors, the CP decomposition is unique under mild conditions. This means the latent patterns found by tensor decomposition are the genuine underlying factors, not just one arbitrary solution among many. In drug discovery, if a tensor of (compound × target × assay) data has a unique CP decomposition, the factors correspond to real biological mechanisms — not mathematical artifacts.

Other Applications

Tensor decompositions analyze brain activity (patient × brain region × time × condition), social network dynamics (user × user × time), hyperspectral images (spatial x × spatial y × wavelength), and knowledge graphs in AI (entity × relation × entity). In signal processing, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) — used to separate mixed audio signals — is a special tensor decomposition problem. Tensor methods are how modern data science handles the complexity of high-dimensional structured data.

Conclusion

Tensors extend matrices to higher dimensions, naturally representing data with multiple interacting factors. CP decomposition expresses a tensor as a sum of interpretable rank-1 patterns; Tucker decomposition generalizes SVD to multiple modes simultaneously. Unlike matrix decompositions, tensor decompositions are often unique — the discovered patterns correspond to genuine underlying structure, not mathematical ambiguity. From Netflix recommendations to brain imaging to drug discovery, tensor decompositions extract the hidden geometry of high-dimensional data.
