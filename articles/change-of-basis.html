Change of Basis: Choosing the Right Coordinate System

#105 of 156 January 3, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

A doctor reading an MRI scan doesn't think in terms of the machine's raw measurements — she thinks in terms of tissue types: bone, muscle, fluid. An audio engineer doesn't think in terms of the raw electrical signal from a microphone — he thinks in terms of frequencies: bass, midrange, treble. In both cases, someone has changed the language of the problem. In mathematics, this is called a change of basis — and it can turn an impossible problem into a simple one.

What Is a Basis?

A basis is a set of reference directions that you use to describe everything else. The standard basis in 2D is the directions right (called î) and up (called ĵ). Any point or vector in the plane can be described using those two directions: "3 right and 2 up" gives you the point (3, 2). That's the basis you learned in school — but it's not the only one, and it's not always the best one.

Imagine you're studying data that naturally lies along a diagonal — say, a dataset where taller people also tend to weigh more, so the cloud of data points stretches diagonally on the height-weight graph. The standard "right and up" coordinate system cuts across that diagonal awkwardly. If you rotated your axes so one axis ran along the diagonal of the data, all the interesting variation would be captured in just one number instead of two. That rotation is a change of basis.

How the Math Works

Suppose your new basis vectors are v₁ and v₂ — two directions that feel more natural for your problem. To convert any point from the old coordinates to the new ones, you build a matrix P whose columns are v₁ and v₂, then multiply:

New coordinates = P⁻¹ × (old coordinates) P = matrix whose columns are your new basis vectors P⁻¹ = the inverse of P (it "undoes" the change) In plain English: P⁻¹ decomposes any vector into how much of v₁ and how much of v₂ it contains.

Going back the other way — from new coordinates to old — you just multiply by P instead of P⁻¹. The two matrices are inverses of each other, like a currency exchange that converts dollars to euros and back.

The Payoff: Diagonal Matrices

Here's where change of basis becomes magical. Many transformations — rotations, stretchings, projections — look complicated in standard coordinates. But in the right basis (specifically, the basis of eigenvectors), the same transformation becomes trivial: it just multiplies each basis direction by a number. The matrix becomes diagonal — zeros everywhere except the main diagonal.

A diagonal matrix is the easiest matrix to work with. Computing its powers, its inverse, or its effect on a vector requires almost no work. Change of basis is how you reach that simplicity — find the right language for the problem, and the problem solves itself.

Back to the MRI

An MRI machine measures signals in "frequency space" — how strongly different radio frequencies resonate in each voxel (tiny cube of tissue). To produce an image, the machine changes basis from frequency space back to physical space. The mathematical tool that does this is the Fourier Transform — which is itself a change of basis from physical position to frequency. Without a change of basis, MRI images would be incomprehensible collections of oscillating numbers rather than recognizable images of anatomy.

Other Applications

Change of basis underlies Principal Component Analysis — the technique that finds the most important directions in a dataset by rotating to the eigenvector basis. It's used in quantum mechanics, where changing to the right basis reveals which energy states a particle can occupy. In computer graphics, every time a 3D object is viewed from a different camera angle, the coordinates of every point are converted from world space to camera space — a change of basis.

Conclusion

The coordinate system you use to describe a problem is a choice, not a necessity. Changing to the right basis can transform a tangled, complicated description into a clean, simple one. Whether you're imaging the human body, analyzing a dataset, or designing a video game, the ability to switch coordinate systems — to reframe the problem — is one of the most powerful tools in applied mathematics. The math is in the matrix; the art is in knowing which basis to choose.
