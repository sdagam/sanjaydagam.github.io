Generative Models: The Mathematics of Creating Data

#116 of 156 March 21, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Type a text prompt and watch a photorealistic image appear from nothing. Ask an AI to write a song in the style of Bach, and it produces four-part harmony that sounds genuinely Baroque. These feel like magic, but they're mathematics — specifically, the mathematics of learning a probability distribution and then sampling from it. Generative models are systems that learn the statistical structure of data and use that structure to create new, realistic examples. Understanding how they work demystifies one of the most remarkable technologies of our time.

The Core Idea: Learning a Distribution

Every collection of data — photographs, songs, text, protein structures — has a hidden statistical structure. Real photographs cluster in a tiny fraction of all possible pixel arrangements. Most arrangements of pixels look like static, not faces or landscapes. Generative models learn where the real data lives in this enormous space, and then they sample from those regions to create new examples that look real.

Think of it like this: real English sentences occupy a specific region of "all possible character sequences." Generative language models learn the shape of that region so well that they can produce new sequences that fall in the same region — grammatical, sensible English sentences that have never been written before.

Variational Autoencoders: Compress and Expand

A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) learns a compact representation of data by training two networks simultaneously. An encoder compresses each input into a small vector of numbers — the "latent code" — that captures the essential structure. A decoder expands that code back into a full output. The magic: the latent codes are constrained to follow a simple probability distribution (usually a Gaussian bell curve).

VAE objective: Minimize: reconstruction error + KL divergence Reconstruction error: how different is the decoded output from the input? KL divergence: how different is the learned latent distribution from a Gaussian? KL(q || p) = Σ q(z) log(q(z)/p(z)) This measures the "distance" between two probability distributions. A small KL divergence means the latent codes are nearly Gaussian. In plain English: the model must both reproduce its inputs accurately AND arrange the latent codes in an organized, continuous space.

Once trained, you can generate new images by sampling a random point from the Gaussian, passing it through the decoder, and getting a realistic output. Because the latent space is continuous and well-organized, you can also interpolate between two images — a face morphing smoothly from one person to another — by moving between their latent codes.

GANs: A Two-Player Game

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) take a different approach. Two networks compete. The generator creates fake samples trying to fool a discriminator. The discriminator tries to distinguish real from fake. As they compete, the generator gets better at creating realistic fakes. The equilibrium of this game — when the discriminator can no longer tell real from fake — is a generator that has learned the true data distribution.

This adversarial training produced the first photorealistic AI-generated faces (the "This Person Does Not Exist" website runs a GAN). It also produced deepfake videos and synthetic medical images for training diagnostic AI without requiring real patient data.

Diffusion Models: Learn to Denoise

The technology behind DALL-E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion is a diffusion model. Training: take a real image, gradually add random noise until it looks like pure static, and train a neural network to reverse each step — to predict what the slightly-less-noisy version of the image looks like. Generation: start with pure random noise and apply the denoising network hundreds of times. Each step removes a little noise, moving from random static toward a realistic image.

Conclusion

Generative models learn the statistical structure of real data — where real examples live in the vast space of possibilities — and use that structure to create new realistic examples. VAEs compress data into organized latent spaces and sample from them. GANs pit a creator against a critic until the creator is indistinguishable from reality. Diffusion models learn to reverse a noise process, step by step, from static to structure. All three are implementations of the same core idea: if you understand a distribution well enough, you can sample from it to create something new.
