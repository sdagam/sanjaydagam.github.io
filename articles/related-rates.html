Related Rates: How Fast Is That Shadow Growing?

#9 March 2, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

A 6-foot person walks away from a 15-foot streetlight at 4 feet per second. How fast is their shadow lengthening? This classic calculus problem illustrates related rates—using derivatives to connect changing quantities through geometric or physical relationships.

Setting Up the Problem

Let x represent the person's distance from the light and s represent the shadow length. The shadow tip is at distance (x + s) from the light. By similar triangles:

15 / (x + s) = 6 / s → 9s = 6x → s = (2/3)x

This relationship holds at every moment—the shadow length is always two-thirds of the person's distance from the light.

Differentiating with Respect to Time

Both x and s change with time. Differentiating s = (2/3)x with respect to time t:

ds/dt = (2/3) × (dx/dt)

We know dx/dt = 4 feet per second. Therefore ds/dt = (2/3) × 4 = 8/3 ≈ 2.67 ft/s. Remarkably, this rate doesn't depend on where the person is.

Another Classic: The Sliding Ladder

A 10-foot ladder leans against a wall. The base slides away at 1 ft/s. How fast does the top slide down when the base is 6 feet from the wall? Let x be the base distance and y the height. The Pythagorean theorem gives x² + y² = 100.

2x(dx/dt) + 2y(dy/dt) = 0 → dy/dt = −0.75 ft/s

At x = 8, y = 6, the top slides at 1.33 ft/s. Near the floor (x = 9.9, y = 1.4), it plummets at over 7 ft/s.

Real-World Applications

Related rates solve practical engineering and physics problems. Air traffic controllers implicitly solve related rates problems when predicting whether flight paths will intersect. Astronomers use similar techniques to calculate how apparent positions of celestial objects change. Economists apply the concept when relating rates of change in interconnected markets.

Conclusion

Related rates exemplify calculus as the mathematics of change. By connecting changing quantities through geometric or physical relationships, we deduce rates that aren't directly observable from rates we can measure. The technique appears throughout physics, engineering, and any field involving dynamic systems.
