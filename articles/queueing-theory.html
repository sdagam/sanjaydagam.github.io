Queueing Theory: The Math of Waiting in Line

#69 of 156 April 26, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

How many checkout lanes does a supermarket need to keep average wait times under two minutes? How many servers should a cloud computing cluster run to handle peak traffic? How many emergency room beds should a hospital maintain? These questions share mathematical structure: a random stream of arrivals, limited service capacity, and queues forming when demand exceeds supply. Queueing theory—pioneered by Danish engineer A. K. Erlang in 1909 while designing telephone networks—provides exact analytical solutions to a surprisingly wide range of waiting-line problems.

The M/M/1 Queue

The simplest queueing model—M/M/1—assumes Poisson arrivals at rate λ, exponential service times with rate μ, a single server, and infinite queue capacity. The steady-state analysis yields elegant closed-form results. The traffic intensity ρ = λ/μ must be less than 1 for stability (otherwise the queue grows without bound). The average number in the system L = ρ/(1−ρ); average waiting time W = 1/(μ−λ) (Little's Law: L = λW). Near capacity (ρ → 1), both queue length and waiting time diverge to infinity—the queuing system becomes unstable long before the server is actually always busy.

M/M/1: ρ = λ/μ < 1 for stability Avg queue length: L = ρ/(1−ρ) Little's Law: L = λW

Little's Law

One of queueing theory's most powerful results is Little's Law: L = λW, where L is the average number of customers in a system, λ is the average arrival rate, and W is the average time a customer spends in the system. This relationship holds for any stable queueing system—any arrival process, any service time distribution, any number of servers. Little's Law requires no distributional assumptions and is therefore universally applicable. It has an elegant proof based on conservation of customers: in steady state, the rate of customers entering must equal the rate leaving.

Little's Law: L = λW Holds for ANY stable queue regardless of arrival/service distributions

M/M/c and Multi-Server Queues

Real service systems have multiple servers. The M/M/c queue has c parallel servers, each with rate μ. A customer waits if all c servers are busy, then goes to the next available server. The Erlang C formula gives the probability of waiting: C(c, ρ) = (ρᶜ/c!)·1/(1−ρ/c) / [Σᵢ₌₀^{c-1} ρⁱ/i! + (ρᶜ/c!)·1/(1−ρ/c)]. Given waiting probability, average wait time is W_q = C(c,ρ)/(cμ−λ). Call centers use this formula to determine staffing levels that achieve target service levels (e.g., 80% of calls answered within 20 seconds).

Traffic intensity: ρ = λ/(cμ) Erlang C: probability a customer must wait = C(c, λ/μ)

Non-Markovian Queues and Simulation

M/M/1 assumptions are rarely met exactly—arrivals may cluster (not Poisson), service times may be deterministic or heavy-tailed. The M/G/1 queue (general service times, single server) has the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula: W_q = λE[S²] / (2(1−ρ)), where E[S²] is the second moment of service time. High service time variability dramatically increases waiting times even at moderate utilization—the reason unpredictable service (like emergency medical care) requires more capacity than predictable service. When analytical solutions are intractable, discrete-event simulation models complex queueing networks computationally.

Applications in Computing

Computer performance engineering applies queueing theory extensively. A web server with request arrival rate λ and average processing time 1/μ has utilization ρ = λ/μ; response times follow M/M/1 predictions when ρ is moderate. Database connection pools, CPU schedulers, and network switches all model as queues. The fundamental insight—that utilization above 70–80% causes disproportionate response time increases due to queueing—guides capacity planning for cloud systems. Jackson networks extend queueing analysis to systems of interconnected queues, modeling multi-tier web architectures where requests flow through multiple service stages.

Conclusion

Queueing theory transforms the everyday experience of waiting into precise mathematical predictions. The M/M/1 model's elegant formulas, Little's universally applicable law, and the Erlang formulas for multi-server systems give engineers and operations managers exact tools for capacity planning, resource allocation, and service level design. The central lesson—that systems near saturation suffer dramatically—guides decisions from hospital staffing to internet infrastructure, making queueing theory one of the most practically impactful branches of applied probability.
