Cryptography: The Mathematics of Secrets

#78 of 156 June 28, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

Every time you connect to a website over HTTPS, your browser and the server perform a cryptographic handshake, establishing a secret key that encrypts all subsequent communication—in milliseconds, invisibly. The security of this exchange rests on mathematical problems believed to be computationally hard: factoring large integers, computing discrete logarithms, or finding short lattice vectors. Modern cryptography is applied mathematics, transforming number theory and algebra into tools that secure the entire digital economy, protect private communications, and authenticate identities at internet scale.

Symmetric Encryption

Symmetric encryption uses the same key for encryption and decryption. The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) is the global standard: it operates on 128-bit blocks using a 128, 192, or 256-bit key, performing 10–14 rounds of substitution, permutation, and mixing operations. The security of AES rests not on mathematical hardness but on confusion and diffusion—obscuring the relationship between plaintext, key, and ciphertext so thoroughly that no attack better than exhaustive key search is known. A 256-bit AES key has 2²⁵⁶ possible values—more than the estimated number of atoms in the observable universe—making brute force impossible.

AES-256: 2²⁵⁶ possible keys ≈ 10⁷⁷ Brute force at 10¹⁸ keys/second: 10⁵⁹ years

Public Key Cryptography: RSA

RSA solves the key distribution problem: how to communicate secretly without first sharing a key. The RSA algorithm exploits the difficulty of factoring the product of two large primes. Generate two large primes p and q; compute N = pq; choose e coprime to (p−1)(q−1); compute d such that ed ≡ 1 (mod (p−1)(q−1)). Public key: (N, e). Private key: d. Encrypt: c = mᵉ mod N. Decrypt: m = cᵈ mod N. Security: to decrypt without d, an attacker must factor N—a problem for which no polynomial-time classical algorithm is known for numbers with thousands of digits.

RSA: public key (N,e), private key d Encrypt: c = mᵉ mod N Decrypt: m = cᵈ mod N Security ← hardness of factoring N = p·q

Elliptic Curve Cryptography

Elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) achieves equivalent security to RSA with much smaller keys—a 256-bit ECC key is as secure as a 3072-bit RSA key. An elliptic curve over a finite field is the set of points satisfying y² = x³ + ax + b (mod p). Point addition defines a group structure; the discrete logarithm problem on this group (given points P and Q = kP, find k) is believed harder per bit than integer factoring. ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) secures Bitcoin transactions; X25519 (a specific elliptic curve) provides the key exchange in TLS 1.3, securing most HTTPS traffic.

ECC: group operations on curve y² = x³ + ax + b (mod p) Security ← hardness of elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem

Hash Functions and Digital Signatures

Cryptographic hash functions—SHA-256, SHA-3—map inputs to fixed-length digests with three key properties: pre-image resistance (can't find input from hash), second pre-image resistance (can't find different input with same hash), and collision resistance (can't find any two inputs with the same hash). Digital signatures combine hashing with asymmetric cryptography: sign by hashing the message and encrypting the hash with your private key; verify by decrypting with the public key and comparing hashes. Digital signatures provide authentication (only the private key holder could have signed) and integrity (any modification changes the hash and invalidates the signature).

Post-Quantum Cryptography

Shor's quantum algorithm can factor integers and compute discrete logarithms in polynomial time on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer—breaking RSA and ECC. The US National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) standardized post-quantum cryptographic algorithms in 2024, including CRYSTALS-Kyber (key encapsulation based on lattice problems) and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (digital signatures). These algorithms rely on problems like Learning With Errors (LWE), believed hard even for quantum computers. The migration to post-quantum cryptography is one of the largest infrastructure challenges in the history of computing, affecting every secure digital communication system.

Conclusion

Cryptography is the mathematical discipline that makes digital trust possible—enabling private communication, authenticated identity, and verifiable transactions without requiring participants to meet in person or share secrets in advance. Its security rests on the assumed difficulty of mathematical problems: integer factorization, discrete logarithm, lattice problems. The transition to post-quantum algorithms underway today illustrates cryptography's perpetual arms race between code makers and code breakers, with the security of the entire digital world depending on which mathematical problems remain hard.
