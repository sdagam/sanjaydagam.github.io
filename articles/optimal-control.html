Optimal Control: Steering Systems to Desired States

#121 of 156 April 25, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

In 1969, the Apollo 11 lunar module had to land on the Moon using a fixed amount of fuel — there was no refueling station. The flight computer had to solve an extraordinarily hard problem in real time: given our current position and velocity, what sequence of engine firings will bring us to a soft landing while using the least possible fuel? Get it wrong and the crew dies. Optimal control is the branch of mathematics that answers this kind of question: given a system with a goal and constraints, find the strategy that achieves the goal most efficiently.

The Setup: A System, a Goal, a Cost

Optimal control problems have three ingredients. First, a dynamical system — something that evolves over time according to differential equations (the spacecraft, a robot arm, a chemical reactor). Second, a goal — a desired final state (soft landing on the Moon, specific product concentration, a robot arm at a target position). Third, a cost to minimize — fuel used, time taken, energy consumed, or some combination.

The control input — the engine throttle, the motor torque, the heating rate — can be adjusted at each moment. The question is: what sequence of control inputs achieves the goal at minimum cost?

Optimal Control Problem: Minimize: ∫₀ᵀ L(x, u) dt + final cost Subject to: dx/dt = f(x, u) (the dynamical system) x(0) = x_start (initial state) x(T) = x_goal (desired final state) x = state (position, velocity, temperature, etc.) u = control input (throttle, torque, heating rate) L = running cost (fuel per second, power consumed, etc.)

Pontryagin's Maximum Principle

The key theorem for solving optimal control problems is Pontryagin's Maximum Principle (1956). It says: at every moment, the optimal control input must maximize (or minimize) a specific quantity called the Hamiltonian — a function that combines the system dynamics, the running cost, and a set of "shadow prices" called costate variables.

Think of costate variables as the hidden value of being in a particular state. For the lunar lander, the costate for velocity represents "how much fuel is it worth spending to change my velocity right now?" When this shadow price is high, spend more fuel to fix velocity; when it's low, let it drift. Pontryagin's principle says the optimal strategy is always to respond to these shadow prices.

The Lunar Landing Solution

For the Apollo fuel-optimal landing problem, Pontryagin's principle leads to a dramatic result: the optimal throttle strategy is "bang-bang" — the engine runs at either maximum thrust or zero thrust, with no intermediate values. There's a single moment when the engine switches from full throttle to off (or the reverse). No gradual throttle adjustment is ever optimal for this type of problem. The Apollo flight computer implemented this switching strategy, finding the precise switch moment to guarantee a soft landing with minimum fuel expenditure.

LQR: The Practical Workhorse

For many engineering applications, the dynamical system is linear and the cost is quadratic (a sum of squared errors and squared control effort). This Linear-Quadratic Regulator (LQR) problem has an exact closed-form solution. The optimal control turns out to be: u = -Kx, where K is a matrix computed by solving a specific matrix equation (the Riccati equation). This means the optimal control is just a linear function of the current state — simple enough to run in real time on a microcontroller.

LQR controls stabilize aircraft (autopilots), balance inverted pendulums (a standard robotics test), maintain satellite orientation, and regulate industrial processes. It's the go-to method whenever a system needs to be steered to a target with minimum energy while tolerating disturbances.

Other Applications

Optimal control manages chemotherapy drug dosing (maximize tumor kill while minimizing toxicity), optimizes power grid dispatch (meet electricity demand at minimum fuel cost), controls autonomous vehicles (plan minimum-time paths to a destination), and guides robotic surgery (precise motion at minimum force). Reinforcement learning (Article #87) can be understood as learning optimal control when the system dynamics are unknown.

Conclusion

Optimal control finds the strategy that achieves a goal at minimum cost for a system governed by differential equations. Pontryagin's Maximum Principle gives the necessary conditions for optimality — leading to the surprising bang-bang result for fuel-optimal problems. The LQR formulation gives an exact solution for linear systems with quadratic costs, producing a simple feedback rule implementable on any processor. From Apollo to autopilots to robotic surgery, optimal control is the mathematics of doing things as efficiently as nature allows.
