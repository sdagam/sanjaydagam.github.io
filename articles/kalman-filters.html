Kalman Filters: Optimal Estimation Under Uncertainty

#115 of 156 March 14, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Your phone's GPS says you're standing on a highway. Your phone knows you can't be — you're not moving fast enough to be in a car. So it uses your speed and direction to adjust the GPS estimate, concluding you're probably on the sidewalk parallel to the highway. Your phone is doing something remarkably sophisticated: it's combining noisy sensor measurements with predictions from a physical model, weighting each by how trustworthy it is, to produce an estimate better than either alone. This process is called a Kalman filter, and it's one of the most practically important algorithms ever invented.

The Problem: Every Measurement Has Noise

GPS signals bounce off buildings and get distorted by the atmosphere. Accelerometers drift over time. Radar measurements jitter. No sensor is perfect. Yet Apollo spacecraft navigated to the Moon, aircraft autopilots maintain precise altitude, and your phone's maps show your location accurately — all despite noisy sensors. The Kalman filter makes this possible by being smart about uncertainty: it tracks not just its best estimate, but how confident it is in that estimate.

Two Sources of Information

The Kalman filter blends two things at each time step. First, a prediction: using a model of how the system moves (a car moving at 60 mph will be about 88 feet further along the road after one second), it predicts where the system should be. Second, a measurement: sensors provide a noisy observation of where the system actually is.

Neither source is perfectly trustworthy. The model might be slightly wrong (there's wind, the road curves). The sensor is noisy. The Kalman filter combines both by weighting them according to their relative uncertainties — giving more weight to whichever source is more reliable at each moment.

Kalman Filter update: K = P_predicted / (P_predicted + R) K = Kalman gain (between 0 and 1) P_predicted = uncertainty in the prediction R = uncertainty in the measurement (sensor noise) New estimate = predicted estimate + K × (measurement - predicted estimate) If K ≈ 1: trust the measurement (prediction is uncertain) If K ≈ 0: trust the prediction (measurement is noisy) If K = 0.5: both equally trustworthy — average them equally

The Kalman gain K is the key. It's automatically computed from the relative uncertainties — you don't have to choose it manually. When your GPS signal is strong (low R), K is large and the filter trusts the GPS reading. When you're in a tunnel (no GPS signal, high R), K drops to zero and the filter relies entirely on its model of your motion.

The Two-Step Cycle

The filter operates in a continuous two-step cycle. Predict: use the model to forecast the next state and update the uncertainty estimate (the prediction becomes less certain over time as errors accumulate). Update: incorporate the new measurement, compute K, correct the prediction, and reduce the uncertainty. Then predict again. This cycle runs dozens of times per second in a GPS receiver.

Why It's Optimal

Rudolf Kálmán proved in 1960 that his filter produces the minimum possible mean squared error estimate — it's not just good, it's provably the best possible combination of model and measurements, given the assumed noise statistics. No other linear algorithm can do better. This mathematical optimality guarantee is why the Kalman filter was immediately adopted by NASA for the Apollo guidance computer, and why it remains the gold standard for estimation problems today.

Other Applications

Kalman filters track aircraft on radar (combining radar position measurements with flight model predictions), stabilize camera footage (estimating true camera motion from the noisy accelerometer signal), guide autonomous vehicles (fusing GPS, lidar, and wheel odometry), control industrial robots (estimating joint positions from noisy encoders), and model financial markets (filtering the true trend from noisy price observations). Any system where you need the best possible estimate from multiple imperfect sources uses some variant of the Kalman filter.

Conclusion

The Kalman filter solves the fundamental problem of estimation under uncertainty: how do you combine a noisy prediction with a noisy measurement to get the best possible estimate? The answer is the Kalman gain — a weight that automatically balances the two sources based on their relative uncertainties. Proven to be mathematically optimal, the filter guided spacecraft to the Moon and now runs in every GPS device, autopilot, and autonomous vehicle. It's the algorithm that makes the noisy real world navigable.
