Diffusion: Why Things Spread from High to Low Concentration

#77 of 156 June 21, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

A drop of ink falls into still water and spreads outward. The scent of a flower drifts across a room. Oxygen moves from the lungs into the bloodstream. Heat flows from a hot pan into a cooler handle. All of these phenomena are governed by diffusion—the net movement of particles or energy from regions of high concentration to regions of low concentration through random molecular motion. The mathematics of diffusion, captured in the diffusion equation, is one of the most important and widely applied partial differential equations in all of science.

Fick's Laws

Fick's first law describes the diffusive flux: J = −D ∇c, where J is the flux (amount per area per time), c is concentration, and D is the diffusion coefficient (m²/s). The negative sign means flux points from high to low concentration—downhill in concentration space. Fick's second law combines this with conservation of mass (∂c/∂t = −∇·J) to give the diffusion equation: ∂c/∂t = D ∇²c. In one dimension: ∂c/∂t = D ∂²c/∂x². This parabolic PDE describes how concentration profiles evolve over time, smoothing out gradients and spreading initial distributions.

Fick's first law: J = −D∇c Diffusion equation: ∂c/∂t = D∇²c = D(∂²c/∂x² + ∂²c/∂y² + ∂²c/∂z²)

The Fundamental Solution

The fundamental solution of the diffusion equation—the solution for a point source of material released at the origin at t = 0—is a Gaussian: c(x,t) = (1/√(4πDt)) exp(−x²/(4Dt)) in one dimension. This solution spreads over time: the width (standard deviation) grows as √(2Dt)—the characteristic diffusion length. In d dimensions, the spread is similar. This Gaussian spreading is the signature of diffusion: the mean displacement is zero (equal probability left and right), but the root-mean-square displacement grows as √t. This √t scaling distinguishes diffusion from ballistic transport (where displacement grows linearly with time).

Fundamental solution: c(x,t) = (4πDt)^{-d/2} exp(−|x|²/4Dt) Diffusion length: σ = √(2Dt) grows as √t

Random Walks and Brownian Motion

Diffusion emerges from the microscopic random motion of molecules. Einstein's 1905 paper on Brownian motion showed that the mean squared displacement of a particle undergoing random collisions grows as ⟨x²⟩ = 2Dt, connecting the macroscopic diffusion coefficient D to microscopic collision rates: D = k_BT/(6πηr) for a sphere of radius r in a fluid of viscosity η. This Einstein relation links thermodynamics (k_BT) to hydrodynamics (η, r)—a deep connection that enabled the first accurate estimate of Avogadro's number from observable quantities. The continuous limit of a random walk is Brownian motion, the mathematical foundation of financial modeling and stochastic calculus.

Einstein: ⟨x²⟩ = 2Dt Einstein relation: D = k_BT/(6πηr) (Stokes-Einstein equation)

Heat Equation

The diffusion equation with thermal conductivity κ and heat capacity ρc_p is the heat equation: ∂T/∂t = α∇²T, where α = κ/(ρc_p) is thermal diffusivity. It has identical mathematical structure to Fick's law—heat diffuses exactly like molecules. Solving the heat equation predicts temperature evolution in conductors, design requirements for heat sinks, and thermal gradients in building envelopes. Fourier's original analysis of heat conduction in the early 19th century led him to develop the Fourier series and transform—tools that transformed mathematics—all to solve this one PDE.

Anomalous Diffusion

Classical diffusion has ⟨x²⟩ ∝ t. Many real systems deviate: anomalous diffusion has ⟨x²⟩ ∝ tᵅ. Sub-diffusion (α < 1) occurs in crowded cellular environments where large molecules obstruct motion—protein diffusion in cell membranes, contaminant transport in fractured rock. Super-diffusion (α > 1, including Lévy flights) occurs when rare but very large steps dominate, seen in animal foraging patterns, financial price jumps, and light transport in certain materials. Fractional calculus—differential equations with non-integer order derivatives—describes anomalous diffusion mathematically, extending the classical diffusion equation to capture these non-Gaussian dynamics.

Conclusion

Diffusion is one of nature's most fundamental transport mechanisms—the inevitable consequence of random molecular motion in a world with concentration gradients. The diffusion equation's solutions, from Gaussian spreading to the heat equation's temperature evolution, describe phenomena across biology, chemistry, physics, and engineering. The mathematical connection between random walks, Brownian motion, and the diffusion equation reveals that macroscopic transport laws emerge from microscopic randomness—a microcosm of statistical mechanics' central theme that collective behavior can be predicted statistically even when individual behavior is unpredictable.
