Terminal Velocity: When Falling Objects Stop Accelerating

#3 January 20, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

Drop a feather and a bowling ball in a vacuum, and they hit the ground together—Galileo's famous insight confirmed by Apollo astronauts on the Moon. But in air, the feather drifts down slowly while the ball plummets. The difference is air resistance, which creates a speed limit for falling objects called terminal velocity.

The Forces at Play

A falling object experiences two opposing forces. Gravity pulls downward with force F = mg, where m is mass and g is gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s² on Earth). Air resistance pushes upward, opposing motion.

F_drag = ½ρv²C_d A

where ρ is air density, v is velocity, C_d is the drag coefficient, and A is cross-sectional area. The critical insight is that drag grows with velocity squared—double your speed, and drag quadruples.

Reaching Terminal Velocity

When you first start falling, velocity is zero, so drag is zero. Gravity accelerates you downward at 9.8 m/s². As speed builds, drag grows, reducing net force and therefore acceleration. Eventually, drag equals gravity and acceleration stops.

v_terminal = √(2mg / ρC_d A)

This formula reveals terminal velocity's dependencies: heavier objects fall faster, larger cross-sections fall slower, and streamlined shapes fall faster.

Skydivers and Cats

For a skydiver in spread-eagle position (mass 75 kg), terminal velocity is approximately 55 m/s or 120 mph. By tucking into a dive, skydivers can exceed 200 mph. Cats have a lower terminal velocity than humans—about 60 mph—due to their small mass relative to their area when they spread their limbs.

Raindrops and Hailstones

Terminal velocity explains why rain doesn't hurt despite falling from thousands of feet. A typical raindrop reaches terminal velocity of only about 9 m/s (20 mph). Hailstones, being denser and sometimes quite large, can have terminal velocities exceeding 100 mph—which is why they cause damage.

Interestingly, raindrops aren't teardrop-shaped. Surface tension makes small drops spherical, while larger drops flatten into pancake shapes due to air pressure on their undersides.

Conclusion

Terminal velocity demonstrates how competing forces reach equilibrium. The mathematics—balancing quadratic drag against linear gravity—appears throughout physics and engineering. From designing parachutes to understanding sedimentation in rivers, terminal velocity connects simple force balance to a remarkable range of real-world phenomena.
