Information Theory: Entropy, Compression, and Channel Capacity

#128 of 156 June 13, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Claude Shannon, a mathematician at Bell Labs, asked a question in 1948 that seemed almost philosophical: what is information, exactly? And how much can you compress it before you lose something? His answers — published in a paper that single-handedly launched the field of information theory — established precise mathematical limits on communication that no technology can exceed. Every data compression algorithm you use (ZIP files, MP3, JPEG) and every communication system (WiFi, cellular networks, fiber optics) operates within limits Shannon proved nearly 80 years ago.

Entropy: Measuring Surprise

Shannon's key insight was that information and surprise are the same thing. A message you were certain to receive contains no information — it's no surprise. A completely unexpected message contains a lot of information. He quantified this with entropy: a measure of the average surprise (or uncertainty) in a source of messages.

Shannon Entropy: H = -Σ p(x) log₂ p(x) (sum over all possible messages x) p(x) = probability of message x log₂ = logarithm base 2 (gives answer in bits) Example: fair coin flip H = -(0.5 × log₂ 0.5 + 0.5 × log₂ 0.5) = 1 bit Biased coin (90% heads, 10% tails): H = -(0.9 × log₂ 0.9 + 0.1 × log₂ 0.1) ≈ 0.47 bits In plain English: a fair coin flip carries 1 bit of information. A biased coin that almost always shows heads carries less — only 0.47 bits — because the outcome is mostly predictable.

Source Coding: You Can't Compress Below Entropy

Shannon proved the Source Coding Theorem: you can compress any message source to an average of H bits per symbol, but no further. Entropy is the fundamental limit on compression. If English text has an entropy of about 1.3 bits per character (it's highly predictable — knowing the previous letters constrains the next), then you can in principle compress English to 1.3 bits per character. ZIP files and other lossless compressors approach this limit.

This is why you can compress a text file significantly but can't compress a truly random file at all — random data has maximum entropy (every bit is equally likely, completely unpredictable), and there's nothing to exploit. Encryption makes files look random precisely to prevent compression-based attacks.

Channel Capacity: The Speed Limit of Communication

Shannon also asked: how fast can you reliably transmit information through a noisy channel — a telephone line, a wireless radio link, a fiber optic cable? His Channel Capacity Theorem gives the answer:

Shannon-Hartley Theorem: C = B × log₂(1 + S/N) C = channel capacity in bits per second (the maximum reliable rate) B = bandwidth in Hz (how wide a frequency range is available) S/N = signal-to-noise ratio (how strong the signal is vs. background noise) Example: WiFi with B = 20 MHz and S/N = 100 (20 dB): C = 20,000,000 × log₂(101) ≈ 133 million bits per second = 133 Mbps

This is a fundamental physical limit — not a technology limitation. No matter how clever your engineering, you cannot reliably transmit information faster than C bits per second on a channel with bandwidth B and signal-to-noise ratio S/N. Modern WiFi and cellular standards (4G, 5G) are designed to approach this limit as closely as practical engineering allows.

Error Correction and the Noisy Channel Theorem

Shannon proved something even more surprising: for any noisy channel, you can transmit information at rates up to the channel capacity with arbitrarily low error — if you use the right coding scheme. This was shocking in 1948; engineers assumed noisy channels would always cause some irreducible errors. Shannon proved errors can be made vanishingly rare, at any rate below capacity. It took decades to find the actual coding schemes (turbo codes, LDPC codes) that approach this limit in practice.

Other Applications

Information theory quantifies the information content of DNA (about 2 bits per base pair), sets limits on how much you can learn from a machine learning dataset, guides the design of efficient neural network architectures, and provides the foundation for cryptography (a perfectly secure cipher must use at least H bits of key per bit of message). It's the mathematics of all communication and storage.

Conclusion

Shannon's information theory gives precise mathematical answers to imprecise philosophical questions. Entropy measures the average information content of a source — its irreducible unpredictability. The source coding theorem says you cannot compress below entropy. The channel capacity theorem gives the maximum reliable communication rate for any noisy channel. These results are not engineering targets — they are mathematical truths that no technology can violate. Every communication system you use operates within limits Shannon proved in 1948.
