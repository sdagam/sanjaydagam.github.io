Maxwell's Equations: The Mathematics of Electromagnetism

#107 of 156 January 17, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

In 1865, James Clerk Maxwell wrote down four equations that unified electricity, magnetism, and light into a single theory. These four equations — which fit on a T-shirt — predicted that radio waves exist before anyone had detected them, explained why the sky is blue, and made possible every wireless technology you use today: WiFi, Bluetooth, cell phones, GPS. They are arguably the most productive four equations in history. And despite their fearsome reputation, the ideas behind them are surprisingly graspable.

The Two Big Ideas

Maxwell's equations are built on two core physical insights. The first: electric charges create electric fields, and changing magnetic fields create electric fields. The second: electric currents and changing electric fields create magnetic fields. These two ideas feed into each other — a changing electric field creates a magnetic field, which if it's also changing creates an electric field, and so on. This self-sustaining cycle is an electromagnetic wave, and it travels through empty space at the speed of light.

Equation 1: Electric Charges Create Fields

Gauss's Law for electricity says: the total electric field pointing outward through any closed surface equals the total electric charge inside. Think of a charged balloon. The electric field radiates outward in all directions, like light from a bulb. The more charge you put inside an imaginary sphere surrounding the balloon, the stronger the field poking through that sphere's surface.

∇ · E = ρ/ε₀ ∇ · E = "divergence of E" — how much electric field spreads outward at each point ρ = electric charge density (charge per unit volume) ε₀ = a constant of nature (permittivity of free space) In plain English: positive charges are sources of electric field (field spreads outward from them). Negative charges are sinks (field points inward toward them).

Equation 2: No Magnetic Monopoles

The magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law says: the total magnetic field through any closed surface is always zero. Unlike electric fields, which start and end on charges, magnetic field lines always form closed loops. There is no such thing as a magnetic "charge" — no isolated north pole without a south pole. Every magnet has two poles, and field lines loop from north to south outside the magnet and south to north inside. Cut a magnet in half and you get two smaller magnets, each with two poles.

Equation 3: Changing Magnetic Fields Create Electric Fields

Faraday's Law says: a changing magnetic field creates a circulating electric field. This is how generators work. Spin a coil of wire in a magnetic field — the field through the coil changes — and that changing field drives electrons around the wire. Electric current flows. Every power plant on Earth — coal, nuclear, wind, hydroelectric — uses this principle to generate electricity. The spinning turbine changes the magnetic flux through a coil, and Faraday's Law turns that into electric power.

Equation 4: Currents and Changing Electric Fields Create Magnetic Fields

Ampère's Law (with Maxwell's addition) says: electric currents create magnetic fields, and — crucially — so do changing electric fields. Maxwell's genius was adding the second part. He realized that without it, the equations were inconsistent. Adding "changing electric fields also create magnetic fields" fixed the inconsistency — and as a consequence, predicted that self-sustaining electromagnetic waves must exist, traveling at the speed of light.

Speed of light predicted by Maxwell: c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀) ≈ 299,792,458 meters per second ε₀ = electric constant (from Equation 1) μ₀ = magnetic constant (from Equation 4) Maxwell computed this from pure theory and matched the experimentally measured speed of light — proving light IS an electromagnetic wave. This was the great unification.

Other Applications

Every antenna — from the one in your phone to the giant dishes of radio telescopes — works by exploiting Maxwell's equations. An oscillating current in a wire creates an oscillating magnetic field, which creates an oscillating electric field, which propagates outward as a radio wave. The receiver reverses the process: the passing wave creates a tiny current in the antenna wire. MRI machines use carefully controlled oscillating magnetic fields (governed by Maxwell's equations) to image the body. Optical fiber communications use light — also an electromagnetic wave — to carry internet data around the world.

Conclusion

Maxwell's four equations describe the complete behavior of electricity and magnetism. Together, they predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves traveling at the speed of light — which turned out to be light itself. From that theoretical prediction in 1865 came radio, television, radar, WiFi, and every wireless communication technology. Four compact mathematical statements, derived from a handful of physical principles, unlocked the electromagnetic age. That's what happens when you find the right equations.
