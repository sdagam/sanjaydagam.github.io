Decision Trees and Random Forests: Learning from Data

#58 of 156 February 8, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

A doctor diagnosing a patient asks a series of questions: Is there fever? Is there cough? Is there shortness of breath? Each answer narrows the diagnosis. This sequential questioning process is exactly what a decision tree does—it learns a series of yes/no questions from data to classify examples or predict outcomes. Random forests take this further, combining hundreds of trees to produce predictions far more accurate and robust than any single tree. Together, they are among the most widely used and interpretable machine learning algorithms.

How Decision Trees Work

A decision tree partitions the feature space into rectangular regions by recursively splitting data based on feature thresholds. At each node, the algorithm selects the feature and threshold that best separates the classes (for classification) or reduces variance (for regression). For classification, 'best' is typically measured by information gain (reduction in entropy) or Gini impurity decrease. The tree grows until leaves are pure (all one class) or a stopping criterion is met. Predictions for new examples follow the tree from root to leaf, with each internal node applying a threshold test.

Gini impurity: G = 1 − Σ pᵢ² Entropy: H = −Σ pᵢ log₂(pᵢ) Split quality: ΔH = H(parent) − Σ (nᵢ/n)·H(child_i)

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

A single decision tree grown to full depth has low bias (it fits training data perfectly) but high variance (it changes dramatically with different training samples)—it overfits. Pruning the tree—cutting back branches that provide little improvement—reduces variance at the cost of some bias. This bias-variance tradeoff is fundamental to all of supervised learning: simpler models have high bias (underfit), complex models have high variance (overfit), and the goal is the sweet spot that minimizes total error on unseen data. Cross-validation estimates this tradeoff empirically to choose the right tree depth.

Total error ≈ Bias² + Variance + Irreducible noise Goal: minimize expected error on new data

Random Forests

Random forests reduce variance by averaging many decorrelated trees. Two sources of randomness make trees decorrelated: bootstrap sampling (each tree trains on a random sample with replacement from the training set—bagging) and feature subsampling (at each split, only a random subset of features is considered—typically √p features for p total). Averaging n trees reduces variance by a factor of n (if trees were independent), while the decorrelation from feature subsampling ensures trees aren't all making the same mistakes. In practice, forests of 100–1000 trees typically converge to near-optimal performance.

Forest prediction: ŷ = (1/B) Σᵢ Tree_i(x) Variance reduction: Var(average) ≈ ρσ² + (1−ρ)σ²/B (ρ = tree correlation)

Feature Importance

A key strength of tree methods is interpretability through feature importance scores. Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI) sums the impurity reduction from all splits on a feature, weighted by the number of samples. Mean Decrease in Accuracy (MDA, or permutation importance) measures how much accuracy drops when a feature's values are randomly shuffled—capturing non-linear and interaction effects missed by MDI. These scores reveal which variables drive predictions, enabling model interpretation and feature selection. This transparency distinguishes forests from neural networks in high-stakes applications like medical diagnosis and credit scoring.

Gradient Boosting

Where random forests train trees independently in parallel, gradient boosting trains them sequentially. Each new tree fits the residuals—the errors—of the ensemble so far. The mathematical insight: fitting residuals is equivalent to gradient descent in function space, where the loss is the prediction error and each tree is a step in the negative gradient direction. XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost are optimized implementations that dominate tabular data competitions. By combining many weak learners (shallow trees) into a powerful ensemble through boosting, these methods achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on structured data tasks.

Conclusion

Decision trees capture the intuitive structure of rule-based reasoning and make it learnable from data. Their extension to random forests and gradient boosting produces some of the most powerful and practical machine learning algorithms available. The combination of high predictive accuracy, built-in feature importance, robustness to outliers, and minimal preprocessing requirements makes tree ensembles the default choice for tabular data problems across industries from finance to healthcare to e-commerce.
