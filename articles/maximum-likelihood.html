Maximum Likelihood Estimation: Fitting Models to Data

#60 of 156 February 22, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

You flip a coin 100 times and get 63 heads. What's your best estimate of the coin's probability of heads? The intuitive answer—63%—is actually the maximum likelihood estimate, the value that makes your observed data most probable. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is the most widely used method for fitting statistical models to data, underpinning linear regression, logistic regression, neural networks, and virtually every parametric statistical method encountered in practice.

The Likelihood Function

Given a statistical model with parameter θ and observed data x₁, x₂, …, xₙ, the likelihood function L(θ) = P(data | θ) measures how probable the data is as a function of the parameter. For independent observations, L(θ) = Π P(xᵢ | θ). Maximum likelihood finds the θ that maximizes L(θ)—the parameter value under which the observed data is most probable. Working with the log-likelihood ℓ(θ) = Σ log P(xᵢ | θ) is equivalent (since log is monotone) and computationally convenient: products become sums, and derivatives are easier.

Log-likelihood: ℓ(θ) = Σᵢ log P(xᵢ | θ) MLE: θ̂ = argmax_θ ℓ(θ)

The Coin Example

For n flips with k heads, P(data | p) = pᵏ(1−p)^{n−k}. The log-likelihood is ℓ(p) = k log p + (n−k) log(1−p). Setting dℓ/dp = k/p − (n−k)/(1−p) = 0 gives p̂ = k/n. For 63 heads in 100 flips, p̂ = 0.63. This confirms the intuitive estimate and illustrates the MLE recipe: write the likelihood, take the log, differentiate, set to zero. For more complex models, this system of equations (the score equations) may require numerical optimization, but the principle is identical.

MLE for Binomial: dℓ/dp = k/p − (n−k)/(1−p) = 0 → p̂ = k/n

Linear Regression as MLE

Ordinary least squares linear regression is MLE under a Gaussian noise assumption. If y_i = β·x_i + ε_i where ε_i ~ N(0, σ²), the log-likelihood is proportional to −Σ(y_i − β·x_i)². Maximizing the log-likelihood means minimizing the sum of squared residuals—exactly what least squares does. This connection reveals why least squares is the 'right' estimator under Gaussian errors: it's the maximum likelihood estimate. Under different noise distributions—Laplace (heavy-tailed) errors, for instance—MLE gives least absolute deviations regression, more robust to outliers.

Properties of MLE

Under regularity conditions, MLE has powerful asymptotic properties. Consistency: the MLE converges to the true parameter as sample size grows. Asymptotic normality: √n(θ̂ − θ) converges in distribution to N(0, I(θ)⁻¹), where I(θ) is the Fisher information matrix. Asymptotic efficiency: the MLE achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound—no consistent estimator has smaller asymptotic variance. These properties make MLE a gold standard for estimation, especially for large samples. For small samples, MLE can be biased, and corrections like restricted MLE (REML) exist for specific models.

Asymptotic normality: √n(θ̂ − θ) → N(0, I(θ)⁻¹) Cramér-Rao: Var(θ̂) ≥ 1/I(θ) for any unbiased estimator

Numerical MLE

Many models lack closed-form MLE solutions. Logistic regression's log-likelihood has no closed form, requiring iterative optimization. The Newton-Raphson method uses the gradient (score) and Hessian of the log-likelihood to update estimates: θ_{t+1} = θ_t − H⁻¹∇ℓ. Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares (IRLS) is an equivalent algorithm that's numerically stable. For complex models like neural networks, stochastic gradient descent on the negative log-likelihood (equivalently, cross-entropy loss) is standard. The universality of MLE lies partly in this: any differentiable probabilistic model can be fit using the same optimization machinery.

Conclusion

Maximum likelihood estimation provides a unified principled framework for parameter estimation across all of statistics. Its logic—find the parameter that makes the observed data most probable—is intuitive, its mathematical properties are excellent, and its computational implementation via optimization is universal. From the simplest coin flip to deep neural networks, MLE is the thread connecting statistical model specification to fitted parameter values, making it arguably the most important estimation concept in applied statistics.
