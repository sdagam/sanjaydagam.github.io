Reinforcement Learning: Learning Through Rewards and Penalties

#87 of 156 August 30, 2025 Group 4 · Jul–Dec 2025

In 2016, a computer program called AlphaGo defeated the world's best Go player — a game so complex that experts believed computers were decades away from mastering it. AlphaGo didn't learn by studying human games. It learned by playing millions of games against itself, winning and losing, adjusting its strategy based on the outcomes. That process — learning from rewards and penalties without being told the right answer — is called reinforcement learning.

The Basic Setup

Reinforcement learning (RL) involves three things: an agent (the learner), an environment (everything the agent interacts with), and a reward signal (feedback on how well the agent is doing). At each step, the agent observes the current state of the environment, takes an action, receives a reward (positive for good outcomes, negative for bad), and moves to a new state. The goal is to find a strategy — called a policy — that maximizes total reward over time.

In AlphaGo's case: the agent is the program, the environment is the Go board, and the reward is +1 for winning and -1 for losing. No human told AlphaGo which moves were good. It discovered good moves entirely through the reward signal of winning and losing.

Agent observes state s → takes action a → receives reward r → moves to new state s' Goal: find policy π(s) = action to take in state s that maximizes total reward

The Explore-Exploit Dilemma

Every RL agent faces a fundamental tension. Should it exploit what it already knows — play the moves it thinks are best? Or should it explore new strategies that might be even better? Too much exploitation and the agent gets stuck in mediocre strategies. Too much exploration and it wastes time on bad moves. AlphaGo solved this elegantly using tree search: explore many possible move sequences, but focus more time on sequences that look promising.

Q-Learning: Scoring Every Move

The most common RL algorithm is Q-learning. It learns a score — called a Q-value — for every (state, action) pair. Q(s, a) is the expected total future reward from taking action a in state s and then playing optimally afterward. Once you know Q-values, the best policy is simple: always pick the action with the highest Q-value in the current state.

Q(s, a) ← Q(s, a) + learning_rate × [reward + best_future_Q - current_Q] In English: update your estimate toward what you actually experienced

The update rule nudges Q-values toward reality: if you expected a move to be worth 5 points but it led to a situation worth only 2 points, decrease its Q-value. Over millions of games, Q-values converge to accurate estimates of each move's true worth.

Deep RL: When States Are Images

Q-learning works perfectly when there are few states to track. But Go has more board positions than atoms in the universe — you can't store Q-values in a table. AlphaGo used a neural network to approximate Q-values from raw board images. This combination — deep learning plus reinforcement learning — is called deep RL, and it's what enabled the breakthrough. The same approach taught computers to play Atari games from raw screen pixels, to control robotic hands, and to optimize industrial processes.

Other Applications

RL is now used to train chatbots to give more helpful responses (the reward signal is human approval ratings), optimize traffic light timing to reduce congestion, design drug molecules by rewarding high predicted efficacy, and control the plasma shape in nuclear fusion reactors. Anywhere a sequence of decisions leads to eventual outcomes that can be scored, RL is a candidate solution.

Conclusion

Reinforcement learning learns from experience — rewards and penalties — without being told the right answer. It's how AlphaGo mastered Go, how robots learn to walk, and how recommendation systems learn your preferences. The mathematical framework is clean: define states, actions, and rewards; use Q-values to score each action; update estimates from experience. The power comes from applying this simple loop millions of times until the agent discovers strategies no human would have thought to try.
