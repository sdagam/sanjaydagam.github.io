Finite Element Analysis: Simulating the Physical World

#64 of 156 March 22, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

Before a single component is manufactured, engineers at Boeing simulate every stress on a new aircraft wing. Before a bridge is built, structural engineers compute deflections under traffic loads. Before a hip implant is designed, biomedical engineers analyze stress concentrations in bone-implant contact. All of these analyses use finite element analysis (FEA)—the dominant numerical method for solving partial differential equations over complex geometries. FEA turns continuous physical problems into large systems of linear equations that computers can solve.

The Core Idea: Discretization

FEA divides a complex geometry into many small, simple shapes—finite elements (triangles and quadrilaterals in 2D; tetrahedra and hexahedra in 3D). Within each element, the solution (displacement, temperature, pressure) is approximated by simple polynomial basis functions. The behavior of each element is described by a local stiffness matrix relating forces to displacements. Assembling all element matrices yields a global system: Ku = f, where K is the global stiffness matrix, u is the vector of unknown displacements at all nodes, and f is the applied load vector. Solving this linear system gives approximate displacements throughout the structure.

Variational Formulation

FEA is grounded in variational calculus. The weak form of the governing PDE (e.g., the elasticity equations) is derived by multiplying by a test function and integrating by parts—reducing the smoothness requirements on the solution and enabling natural boundary conditions. For linear elasticity, minimizing the total potential energy (strain energy minus work done by loads) over all admissible displacement fields gives the equilibrium state. This variational principle—find u minimizing ∫(½ε:C:ε − f·u) dV—is equivalent to the governing PDEs but better suited to numerical approximation because it only requires square-integrable (not twice-differentiable) functions.

Mesh Quality and Convergence

The mesh—the division of the geometry into elements—critically affects accuracy. Distorted elements (very elongated or with obtuse angles) degrade accuracy. Mesh refinement (using more, smaller elements) improves accuracy: the error in the FEA solution decreases as a power of element size h, typically O(h^p) where p depends on the polynomial order of the basis functions. Adaptive mesh refinement concentrates elements where the solution varies rapidly (stress concentrations, crack tips) and uses coarser elements elsewhere, achieving high accuracy with fewer total elements. This automatic refinement is essential for problems with stress singularities.

Nonlinear and Dynamic Analysis

Linear FEA assumes small displacements and linear material behavior. Nonlinear FEA handles large deformations (rubber, biological tissue), plasticity (metal forming), contact mechanics (gear teeth, joints), and geometric nonlinearity (buckling). These require iterative Newton-Raphson solvers that linearize the nonlinear equations at each step and update the solution incrementally. Dynamic FEA adds mass and damping matrices to model structural vibration, impact, crash simulation, and earthquake response. Explicit time integration (suitable for fast dynamic events like crash) and implicit methods (suitable for slower dynamics) trade computational cost against stability.

Applications Across Engineering

FEA has transformed every branch of engineering. Civil engineers simulate bridge deflections, dam seepage, and soil-foundation interaction. Automotive engineers simulate crash safety, fatigue life, and NVH (noise, vibration, harshness). Aerospace engineers analyze composite laminates, aeroelastic flutter, and thermal stresses in jet engine components. Biomedical engineers model bone remodeling, arterial wall mechanics, and device-tissue interaction. Electronic packaging engineers predict thermal stresses from coefficient-of-thermal-expansion mismatches. The $10 billion simulation software market is dominated by FEA codes—ANSYS, Abaqus, NASTRAN—that have become indispensable design tools.

Conclusion

Finite element analysis is numerical mathematics applied to engineering at industrial scale. By discretizing continuous domains into manageable elements, assembling global systems from local ones, and solving large linear (or nonlinear) systems, FEA makes the intractable tractable. It allows engineers to predict failure before it occurs, optimize designs without physical prototypes, and understand physical behavior in conditions impossible to test experimentally. The mathematics—variational principles, linear algebra, numerical analysis—becomes invisible infrastructure enabling the physical intuition of engineering design.
