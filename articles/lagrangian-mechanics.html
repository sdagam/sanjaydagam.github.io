Lagrangian Mechanics: Solving Motion with Energy

#113 of 156 February 28, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

How does a double pendulum move — a pendulum with another pendulum hanging from its end? Using Newton's laws directly, you'd need to track all the forces: gravity on both bobs, tension in both rods, and the complicated way the two pendulums pull on each other. The algebra becomes a nightmare. In 1788, Joseph-Louis Lagrange invented a different approach: ignore the forces entirely and focus on energy instead. His method, called Lagrangian mechanics, turns this nightmare into a calculation you can follow step by step — and it works for any mechanical system, no matter how complicated.

The Key Idea: Energy Over Forces

Newton's approach asks: what forces are acting, and what acceleration do they cause? For a simple pendulum, this works fine. For a double pendulum, a robot arm with six joints, or a spinning top, the force analysis becomes intractable — too many interacting components.

Lagrange's approach asks instead: what is the system's kinetic energy (energy of motion) and what is its potential energy (stored energy)? The difference between these two is called the Lagrangian, written L:

Lagrangian: L = T - V T = kinetic energy (energy of motion) For a simple mass: T = ½mv² (half times mass times speed squared) V = potential energy (stored energy) For gravity near Earth: V = mgh (mass times gravity times height) In plain English: L measures how much more kinetic energy a system has than potential energy at each moment.

The Euler-Lagrange Equation

Once you have L, you plug it into the Euler-Lagrange equation — a recipe that automatically produces the equations of motion for any coordinate you choose.

Euler-Lagrange equation: d/dt(∂L/∂q̇) - ∂L/∂q = 0 q = any coordinate describing position (angle, distance, etc.) q̇ = the rate of change of that coordinate (velocity) ∂L/∂q = how L changes if you change the position slightly ∂L/∂q̇ = how L changes if you change the velocity slightly In plain English: plug in your Lagrangian, take some derivatives, and the equation of motion falls out automatically.

For a simple pendulum with angle θ, this procedure gives: d²θ/dt² = -(g/L)sinθ — exactly Newton's result. But for the double pendulum, you choose two angles θ₁ and θ₂, write T and V in terms of both angles, and apply the Euler-Lagrange equation to each. Two equations of motion pop out automatically, without ever writing down a single force or tension.

Why Energy Is Easier Than Forces

Forces are vectors — they have direction, and their directions depend on the current configuration of the system in ways that can be hard to track. Energy is a scalar — a single number. Adding up kinetic and potential energies for each part of a system is straightforward arithmetic, even for complicated systems.

Lagrangian mechanics is also flexible about coordinates. Newton requires you to work in x, y, z. Lagrange lets you choose whatever coordinates are convenient — angles, distances along curves, ratios. For a pendulum, angle θ is much more natural than x and y separately. This freedom to choose coordinates — each set potentially simplifying the problem — is Lagrange's greatest practical gift.

Conservation Laws Fall Out Naturally

A beautiful bonus: if the Lagrangian doesn't depend on a particular coordinate q (only its rate of change q̇), then the quantity ∂L/∂q̇ is automatically conserved — it never changes. This is Noether's theorem in disguise. If L doesn't depend on the angle around a rotation axis, angular momentum is conserved. If L doesn't depend on time, total energy is conserved. Conservation laws that physicists discovered empirically fall out automatically from the Lagrangian framework.

Other Applications

Lagrangian mechanics is used to analyze robot arm motion (each joint angle is a natural coordinate), spacecraft attitude control (orientation described by three angles), particle physics (quantum field theory is built on Lagrangians), and general relativity (Einstein's equations follow from a specific Lagrangian for spacetime curvature). Anywhere the force-based approach becomes unwieldy, Lagrangians provide an elegant alternative.

Conclusion

Lagrangian mechanics replaces forces — which are hard to track in complicated systems — with energy, which is easy to add up. By computing kinetic minus potential energy and applying the Euler-Lagrange equation, you get the equations of motion for any mechanical system automatically, in whatever coordinates are most convenient. It's the method that makes double pendulums, robot arms, and spacecraft tractable — and it's the foundation on which modern theoretical physics is built.
