Temporal Networks: How Time Changes Everything

#92 of 156 October 4, 2025 Group 4 · Jul–Dec 2025

In 2014, an Ebola outbreak in West Africa became the deadliest in history, killing over 11,000 people. Epidemiologists modeling the spread used contact networks — graphs of who was in contact with whom. But standard network models gave predictions that were badly wrong. The missing ingredient was time: not just who contacted whom, but when. Adding timestamps to a network changes almost everything about what can spread and how fast.

The Problem with Snapshots

A standard network is a snapshot: connect Person A to Person B if they ever have contact. But if A visits B on Monday and B visits C on Sunday, a disease can't travel from A to C through B — that would require going backward in time. In a static network, you'd see a path A→B→C and predict the disease could spread that way. In a temporal network, you track when each contact happens and see the path is causally blocked.

Static network: A—B—C (path exists, so spread seems possible) Temporal: A→B on Monday, B→C on Sunday (no time-respecting path A to C)

For Ebola, this mattered enormously. Burial ceremonies — where mourners touched the deceased — were a major transmission route. These contacts happened at specific times during a short window. Timing determined everything about who infected whom, and static network models missed these dynamics entirely.

Time-Respecting Paths

A time-respecting path is a sequence of contacts where each contact happens after the previous one. Only these paths can transmit disease or information. The set of people reachable from a starting person via time-respecting paths can be dramatically smaller than what a static network suggests. In the Ebola network, careful temporal analysis showed that certain funeral ceremonies created brief "super-spreading windows" — time periods when one contact would lead to chains reaching dozens of people. Outside those windows, the same contacts would reach almost no one.

Burstiness Changes Everything

Human contact isn't uniform — we communicate in bursts. You might exchange ten texts with a friend in ten minutes, then nothing for three days. This burstiness slows spreading compared to what you'd predict from average contact rates. Here's why: if two people have 10 contacts per week but all clustered in one hour, and a disease has a 24-hour infectious window, those 10 contacts effectively count as one transmission opportunity — not ten. Static models using average rates would overestimate spread dramatically.

In the Ebola data, researchers found that accounting for burstiness in burial ceremony attendance reduced the predicted number of people infected in each cluster by roughly 40% compared to static models. That's a big enough error to make intervention strategies fail.

Better Interventions

Temporal network analysis led to better targeted interventions during Ebola. Instead of quarantining everyone who had any contact with an infected person, health workers focused on contacts during the patient's infectious window — typically the 48 hours before death and the burial ceremony. Contacts before that window were irrelevant. This more precise targeting allowed limited quarantine resources to be used more effectively and reduced the number of people unnecessarily isolated.

Other Applications

Temporal networks improve models of information spreading on social media (a tweet's reach depends on when followers are online), disease spread in schools (contact patterns differ by time of day and day of week), and financial contagion (market shocks propagate along chains of transactions that happen in sequence).

Conclusion

Adding timestamps to a network isn't just a refinement — it fundamentally changes what can spread and how. Time-respecting paths, bursty contact patterns, and infectious windows all interact to produce spreading dynamics that static models get badly wrong. The Ebola epidemic showed in tragic terms how much that matters. Temporal networks give epidemiologists a more honest model of how disease actually moves through a population, enabling interventions that work in the real world rather than in the simplified world of static graphs.
