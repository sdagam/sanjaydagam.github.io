Quadratic Forms and Conic Sections: Curves as Matrices

#117 of 156 March 28, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Every planet in our solar system travels in an ellipse. Every satellite dish is a parabola. Every cooling tower at a nuclear plant is a hyperbola. These three curves — ellipse, parabola, hyperbola — are the conic sections, and they've been studied for over two thousand years. What's less well known is that each of these curves is secretly a matrix in disguise. The connection between curves and matrices, called quadratic forms, is one of the most beautiful bridges in mathematics — and it's directly useful for everything from optimizing machine learning models to designing antennas.

Conic Sections: A Quick Tour

An ellipse is a stretched circle — like a circle seen at an angle. The orbit of Earth around the Sun is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus. A parabola is the curve followed by a ball thrown through the air (ignoring air resistance). A satellite dish is a parabola because any signal arriving parallel to the axis reflects through a single point — the focus — where the receiver sits. A hyperbola appears in navigation systems that use time differences between radio signals from two stations.

The Equation Behind All Three

All three curves can be written using an equation of the form ax² + bxy + cy² = 1 — a sum of squared and cross terms in x and y. The coefficients a, b, c determine which curve you get. This expression — a polynomial where every term has degree exactly 2 — is called a quadratic form. And it can be written as a matrix equation:

Quadratic form: ax² + bxy + cy² = xᵀAx where x = [x, y] (vector of coordinates) A = [[a, b/2], (a 2x2 symmetric matrix) [b/2, c]] xᵀAx means: write x as a row, multiply by A, multiply by x as a column. The result is a single number — the quadratic form's value. Example: x² + 4xy + y² = xᵀ[[1,2],[2,1]]x

The matrix A completely encodes the quadratic form. Different matrices give different curves. The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A determine the shape and orientation of the curve.

Eigenvalues Determine the Shape

Here's the beautiful connection. Change to the eigenvector basis of A (Article #105: change of basis) and the cross term bxy disappears. The equation becomes λ₁u² + λ₂v² = 1, where λ₁ and λ₂ are the eigenvalues and u, v are coordinates along the eigenvectors.

After changing to eigenvector basis: λ₁u² + λ₂v² = 1 Both eigenvalues positive → ellipse (both axes real) One eigenvalue zero → parabola (one axis goes to infinity) Eigenvalues opposite signs → hyperbola (one term positive, one negative) The eigenvectors point along the axes of the curve. The eigenvalues determine the axis lengths: axis length = 1/√λ

This is why eigenvalues matter for optimization: the loss function in machine learning is often a quadratic form near its minimum. If all eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix (the matrix of second derivatives) are positive, the loss function is bowl-shaped — an ellipse in 2D — and gradient descent converges to the bottom. If eigenvalues differ greatly in size, the bowl is very elongated — a thin ellipse — and gradient descent zigzags slowly. This is why optimization algorithms account for eigenvalue structure when choosing step sizes.

Planetary Orbits

Kepler discovered empirically that planets move in ellipses. Newton proved it mathematically from gravity. The orbit's shape — how elongated the ellipse is — is captured by a number called eccentricity, which is directly related to the eigenvalue ratio of the quadratic form describing the orbit. Earth's orbit is nearly circular (eigenvalues nearly equal). Halley's Comet has a very elongated ellipse (eigenvalues very different in size) — it swings close to the Sun every 75 years and spends decades in the outer solar system.

Conclusion

Quadratic forms connect curves to matrices: every conic section — ellipse, parabola, hyperbola — corresponds to a specific symmetric matrix. The eigenvalues of that matrix determine whether the curve is circular, elongated, or open. This connection is not just beautiful — it's practical. It explains why gradient descent sometimes converges slowly (badly conditioned Hessian), why satellite dishes have a specific shape (parabola focuses parallel signals), and why planetary orbits are ellipses (quadratic gravitational potential). Curves and matrices speak the same language.
