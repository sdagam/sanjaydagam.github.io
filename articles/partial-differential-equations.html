Partial Differential Equations: The Heat and Wave Equations

#124 of 156 May 16, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

When a wildfire burns through a forest, heat spreads outward from the fire front — faster where the temperature gradient is steeper, slower where it's gradual. Predicting how the fire will spread requires knowing the temperature at every point in space and every moment in time. This is not one number changing over time (like cooling coffee from Article #106) — it's a function of both space and time simultaneously. The mathematics that handles this is partial differential equations, and it governs not just heat but sound, water waves, light, earthquakes, and the weather.

What Makes It "Partial"?

An ordinary differential equation (ODE) has one independent variable — usually time. The temperature of a coffee cup depends only on time: T(t). A partial differential equation (PDE) has multiple independent variables — typically space and time. The temperature of a steel bar depends on both position x along the bar and time t: T(x, t). When you take a derivative of T, you must specify which variable you're differentiating with respect to — that's what "partial" means.

Partial derivative notation: ∂T/∂t = how temperature changes over time at a fixed location ∂T/∂x = how temperature changes across space at a fixed moment ∂²T/∂x² = how the temperature gradient itself changes across space (the "curvature" of the temperature profile)

The Heat Equation

Heat flows from hot regions to cold regions. The key insight: the rate at which temperature changes at any point is proportional to the curvature of the temperature profile at that point. Where the temperature profile is concave (like a valley — hotter neighbors on both sides), heat flows in and temperature rises. Where it's convex (like a hill — cooler neighbors on both sides), heat flows out and temperature falls.

Heat equation: ∂T/∂t = α · ∂²T/∂x² ∂T/∂t = rate of temperature change over time (what we want to predict) ∂²T/∂x² = curvature of temperature across space (what drives the change) α = thermal diffusivity (a material property — how fast heat spreads) In plain English: temperature rises where the profile curves up, falls where the profile curves down, and the rate is proportional to α.

This equation predicts how a hot coal seam underground cools over geological time, how your forehead temperature responds to a fever medication, how a chip's temperature responds to processing load, and how a wildfire's heat front spreads through the forest.

The Wave Equation

Sound, guitar strings, ocean waves, seismic waves, and light all satisfy the wave equation. Instead of temperature spreading and smoothing, a wave propagates — carrying a disturbance from one location to another without spreading out (in the ideal case).

Wave equation: ∂²u/∂t² = c² · ∂²u/∂x² u = displacement (height of water, displacement of string, pressure of air) c = wave speed (set by material properties) ∂²u/∂t² = acceleration of displacement at each point ∂²u/∂x² = spatial curvature of the displacement In plain English: acceleration at each point is proportional to the curvature of the displacement profile. This produces waves that travel at speed c without changing shape.

Fourier's Gift: Solving Both Equations

Fourier (Article #89) discovered that both equations can be solved using the same approach: decompose the initial condition (initial temperature distribution, or initial wave shape) into sine and cosine components. Each component evolves independently and simply — decaying exponentially for the heat equation, oscillating at a fixed frequency for the wave equation. Sum the components back up for the full solution. This works because sines and cosines are the "natural modes" of both equations.

Other Applications

The heat equation governs the diffusion of pollution in groundwater, the spread of chemicals through tissue, and the pricing of financial options (the Black-Scholes equation in finance is a modified heat equation). The wave equation governs how seismographs detect distant earthquakes, how ultrasound images the body, and how antennas broadcast signals. Meteorological computer models solve PDEs for temperature, pressure, and wind velocity at millions of grid points to produce weather forecasts.

Conclusion

Partial differential equations describe how things change across both space and time simultaneously. The heat equation says temperature changes at each point in proportion to the curvature of its spatial profile — modeling fire spread, fever, and chip cooling. The wave equation says acceleration is proportional to spatial curvature — producing traveling waves of sound, light, and seismic vibration. Fourier analysis solves both by decomposing the problem into independent modes. PDEs are the mathematical language of the physical world in all its spatial and temporal complexity.
