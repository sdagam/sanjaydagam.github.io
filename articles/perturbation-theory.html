Perturbation Theory: Solving Almost-Solvable Problems

#119 of 156 April 11, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

The orbit of Mercury around the Sun doesn't quite follow the path that Newton's gravity predicts. It drifts — the point of closest approach slowly rotates around the Sun, completing a full rotation every 3 million years. For a century, astronomers couldn't explain this discrepancy. When Einstein's general relativity finally did explain it, the calculation used a technique called perturbation theory: start with a simpler problem you can solve exactly, then add the complication as a small correction. This approach is used across physics, engineering, and mathematics whenever exact solutions are out of reach.

The Core Idea: Small Changes, Systematic Corrections

Most real problems are too complicated to solve exactly. But many real problems are close to a simpler problem that you can solve. Perturbation theory exploits this: solve the simple problem exactly, then systematically calculate how the solution changes when you add the complication.

The key requirement is that the complication be small — a "perturbation." If you're calculating the trajectory of a spacecraft past Jupiter, you solve the two-body problem (Sun and spacecraft) exactly, then add Jupiter's gravitational pull as a small perturbation. If you're analyzing the energy levels of an atom in a weak magnetic field, you solve the zero-field problem exactly and add the magnetic effect as a perturbation.

Perturbation setup: H = H₀ + εH₁ H = the full problem (hard to solve) H₀ = the simpler version (exactly solvable) H₁ = the complication (the perturbation) ε = a small number measuring how large the perturbation is Perturbed solution: E = E₀ + εE₁ + ε²E₂ + ... E₀ = exact solution to the simple problem εE₁ = first correction (linear in ε) ε²E₂ = second correction (smaller still)

The solution is expressed as a series of corrections, each one proportional to a higher power of ε. Since ε is small, each correction is smaller than the last. Usually the first one or two corrections are enough for practical accuracy.

Mercury's Orbit

In Newtonian gravity, Mercury's orbit is a perfect ellipse that never rotates. General relativity adds a small correction to the gravitational force — a perturbation that is tiny near Earth but significant close to the Sun, where Mercury orbits. When Einstein computed the first-order perturbation to Mercury's orbital equations, he got a precession of 43 arc-seconds per century — exactly matching the unexplained astronomical observations. This was one of the first experimental confirmations of general relativity.

The Hydrogen Atom in a Magnetic Field

Hydrogen's energy levels — the specific energies electrons can have — are exactly solvable from quantum mechanics. When you apply a magnetic field, the levels split (the Zeeman effect). The magnetic field is a perturbation. First-order perturbation theory predicts exactly how each energy level splits and by how much — a calculation done in a few lines that matches experimental measurements to extraordinary precision.

First-order energy correction: E₁ = ⟨ψ₀|H₁|ψ₀⟩ ψ₀ = the wave function solving the unperturbed problem H₁ = the perturbation (e.g., the magnetic field interaction) ⟨ψ₀|H₁|ψ₀⟩ = an average of H₁ weighted by ψ₀² In plain English: compute how much the perturbation "feels like" to the unperturbed system, on average.

When Perturbation Theory Breaks Down

Perturbation theory works when ε is genuinely small. When the perturbation is large — when you're not close to a solvable case — the series of corrections diverges (gets bigger, not smaller), and perturbation theory fails. Strong nuclear force calculations in particle physics, for instance, can't use perturbation theory because the coupling constant isn't small. Physicists must use completely different methods (lattice QCD, numerical simulation) for those cases.

Other Applications

Perturbation theory is used in structural engineering (calculating how a building's vibration frequencies change when extra weight is added), economics (analyzing how a policy change affects market equilibrium given small changes in supply or demand), and aerodynamics (calculating drag on a wing shape that's slightly different from a known solvable shape). Any "almost solvable" problem — where the exact solution is just out of reach but a nearby problem is tractable — is a candidate for perturbation theory.

Conclusion

Perturbation theory is the mathematician's way of squeezing answers out of problems that are almost — but not quite — solvable. Start with the exact solution to a simpler problem, then compute corrections one by one, each smaller than the last. It explained Mercury's orbital drift, predicted the Zeeman splitting of atomic energy levels, and guides engineers designing slightly irregular structures. The art is recognizing when your hard problem is a small perturbation away from an easy one — and then exploiting that closeness systematically.
