Stability Analysis: When Do Solutions Stay Bounded?

#129 of 156 June 20, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

In 1999, an experimental footbridge in London — the Millennium Bridge — opened to the public. Within two days it was closed. When pedestrians walked across it, the bridge swayed slightly. People instinctively adjusted their gait to match the sway. Their synchronized footsteps made the sway larger. Larger sway caused more synchronization. The oscillations grew rapidly — a dangerous positive feedback loop. The bridge had passed through an instability: a small disturbance grew rather than dying away. Stability analysis is the mathematical tool that predicts whether a system will return to equilibrium after a disturbance or spiral away from it.

Stable vs. Unstable Equilibria

An equilibrium is a state where nothing changes — the right-hand side of the differential equation equals zero. But equilibria come in flavors. A stable equilibrium returns to itself after small disturbances — like a ball at the bottom of a bowl. An unstable equilibrium grows away from itself — like a ball balanced on top of a hill. Disturb it slightly and it rolls off, accelerating away.

The Millennium Bridge had an equilibrium (no oscillation) that was stable in still conditions but became unstable once pedestrians started synchronizing. Identifying this instability in advance — through stability analysis — would have prevented the expensive redesign.

Linearization: Analyzing Small Disturbances

To analyze stability near an equilibrium, the standard technique is linearization: assume the disturbance is small and approximate the nonlinear differential equation by a simpler linear one that's valid near the equilibrium point.

For a system: dx/dt = f(x) with equilibrium x* (where f(x*) = 0) Linearize around x*: Let u = x - x* (u is the small disturbance) du/dt ≈ J · u where J = ∂f/∂x evaluated at x* (J is the Jacobian matrix — the matrix of all partial derivatives) This linear system has exact solutions: u(t) = Σ cᵢ e^(λᵢt) vᵢ λᵢ = eigenvalues of J, vᵢ = eigenvectors If all eigenvalues have negative real part → disturbances decay → STABLE If any eigenvalue has positive real part → some disturbances grow → UNSTABLE

The eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix are the stability verdict. Negative real parts mean the disturbance decays exponentially — the equilibrium is stable. Positive real parts mean some perturbation grows exponentially — the equilibrium is unstable. Complex eigenvalues (a ± bi) produce oscillations: negative real part means the oscillation decays (stable oscillation), positive real part means growing oscillations (the Millennium Bridge).

The Millennium Bridge Analysis

Engineers modeling the bridge-pedestrian system found that the coupling between pedestrian gait and bridge oscillation had a positive feedback structure. When the bridge sways at a particular frequency, pedestrians synchronize to that frequency (an instability in the human-bridge coupled system). Above a critical pedestrian density, the eigenvalue of the linearized system crossed from negative to positive — the equilibrium became unstable. The fix: add tuned mass dampers (heavy pendulums inside the bridge) that shifted the eigenvalues back into the stable region by adding damping to the relevant oscillation mode.

Lyapunov's Method: Stability Without Solving

For systems where you can't find the eigenvalues (large nonlinear systems), Lyapunov's method provides stability information without solving the equations. Find a Lyapunov function V(x) — a positive function that's zero only at the equilibrium — and check whether it always decreases along solutions. If V decreases everywhere, the equilibrium is stable: the system always moves toward where V is smaller, which means toward the equilibrium.

Energy is the natural Lyapunov function for physical systems. If total energy always decreases (because friction is dissipating it), the system is stable. Lyapunov's method is why "does the system lose energy?" often answers the stability question without any eigenvalue calculation.

Other Applications

Stability analysis certifies that aircraft autopilots won't oscillate, that drug dosing regimens won't create dangerous drug accumulation, that power grids won't cascade into blackouts after a generator fails, and that ecological predator-prey systems return to balance after a disturbance. Unstable equilibria are equally important: knowing an equilibrium is unstable tells engineers where to add damping, add feedback control, or redesign the system before deployment.

Conclusion

Stability analysis determines whether small disturbances from equilibrium grow (unstable) or decay (stable). Linearizing around the equilibrium and computing the eigenvalues of the Jacobian gives the answer: negative real parts mean stable, positive real parts mean unstable. Complex eigenvalues add oscillations. The Millennium Bridge failed because pedestrian-bridge coupling created an unstable eigenvalue. Adding tuned mass dampers moved the eigenvalues back to stability. From bridges to aircraft to power grids, stability analysis is the mathematical safety check that determines whether a system will behave or misbehave when reality doesn't cooperate.
