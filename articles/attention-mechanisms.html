Attention Mechanisms: The Engine Behind Transformers

#93 of 156 October 11, 2025 Group 4 · Jul–Dec 2025

Read this sentence: "The trophy didn't fit in the bag because it was too big." What does "it" refer to — the trophy or the bag? You knew immediately: the trophy. Now consider how hard that is to teach a computer. The word "it" is four words away from "trophy" and two words away from "bag." For decades, AI systems struggled with exactly this kind of long-range connection. Attention mechanisms solved it — and in doing so, triggered the current AI revolution.

The Old Problem

Before attention, language models processed text word by word, passing information forward through a "hidden state" — a fixed-size summary of everything seen so far. By the time the model reached "it," the hidden state had been updated many times and the information about "trophy" had been diluted. Long-range connections got lost. The farther apart two related words were, the harder it was for the model to connect them.

Attention: Looking at Everything at Once

Attention solves this by letting a model look at all words simultaneously when processing each word. When the model processes "it," instead of relying on a compressed history, it computes a relevance score between "it" and every other word in the sentence. "Trophy" gets a high score. "Bag" gets a lower score. "Didn't" gets almost zero. The model then forms its understanding of "it" by taking a weighted average of all words, weighted by those relevance scores. High-relevance words contribute more; irrelevant words barely register.

Relevance score between words i and j: score(i,j) = qᵢ · kⱼ (dot product of query vector for word i and key vector for word j) Attention weight: softmax(scores) → sums to 1, like probabilities

The query and key vectors are learned during training. "It" develops a query vector that points toward large physical objects. "Trophy" and "bag" develop key vectors that describe their physical properties. The dot product is large when a query and key match — when "it" is looking for exactly what "trophy" describes.

The Full Formula

Attention uses three components for each word: a Query (what am I looking for?), a Key (what do I offer?), and a Value (what information do I carry?). The output for each word is a weighted sum of all Value vectors, where the weights come from matching Queries with Keys:

Attention output = softmax(QKᵀ / √d) × V Q: queries (what each word looks for) K: keys (what each word offers) V: values (what each word contributes to the output)

Dividing by √d (the square root of the dimension size) prevents the dot products from getting so large that the softmax collapses to a one-hot distribution — which would effectively ignore all but the highest-scoring word.

Why This Changed AI

The 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" showed that attention — without the word-by-word recurrent processing used before — achieved better results on language translation and could be computed much faster, because all positions are processed in parallel. The resulting Transformer architecture scaled to billions of parameters. GPT, Claude, and every major language model today are built on this foundation. Attention is the core operation running trillions of times per second in data centers worldwide.

Conclusion

Attention mechanisms solve the problem of connecting distant parts of a sentence by computing relevance scores between every pair of words and using those scores to weight what each word pays attention to. The math — dot products, softmax, weighted averages — is simple. The impact was transformative: it gave AI systems the ability to understand context across long distances, unlocking performance that seemed impossible just a decade ago. Understanding attention means understanding why modern AI works.
