Statistical Mechanics: From Atoms to Temperature

#67 of 156 April 12, 2025 Group 3 · Jan–Jun 2025

Temperature is 293 Kelvin. Pressure is 101 kilopascals. These macroscopic quantities describe a gas with ~10²³ molecules whose individual positions and velocities are utterly unknowable. Statistical mechanics bridges the microscopic and macroscopic worlds: using probability and statistics to derive thermodynamic laws from the mechanics of atoms. It explains why gases obey the ideal gas law, why heat flows from hot to cold, and why absolute zero is unreachable—all from nothing more than Newton's laws and the counting of microstates.

The Boltzmann Distribution

A system in thermal equilibrium with a heat bath at temperature T has its microscopic states distributed according to the Boltzmann distribution: P(state with energy E) ∝ e^{−E/k_B T}. Low-energy states are exponentially more probable than high-energy ones; the ratio depends only on the temperature. The partition function Z = Σ e^{−E_i/k_B T} normalizes the distribution and encodes all thermodynamic information: free energy F = −k_B T ln Z, entropy S = −∂F/∂T, average energy U = −∂ ln Z/∂β (where β = 1/k_B T). The Boltzmann distribution is the cornerstone of statistical mechanics.

Boltzmann distribution: P_i = e^{−E_i/k_BT} / Z Partition function: Z = Σᵢ e^{−E_i/k_BT}

The Ideal Gas

For N non-interacting point particles of mass m in a box, the partition function factorizes into N single-particle partition functions. The single-particle partition function integrates over all positions and momenta: Z₁ = V(2πmk_BT/h²)^{3/2}, the thermal de Broglie volume. The resulting thermodynamics gives: average kinetic energy = (3/2)k_BT per particle; pressure P = Nk_BT/V (ideal gas law); heat capacity C_V = (3/2)Nk_B. The equipartition theorem—each quadratic degree of freedom contributes (1/2)k_BT to average energy—follows naturally, explaining why diatomic gases have higher heat capacity than monatomic ones.

Phase Transitions

Statistical mechanics explains phase transitions—the abrupt changes in material properties at critical temperatures. The Ising model—a lattice of binary spins (+1 or −1) with nearest-neighbor interactions—is the simplest model exhibiting a phase transition. Below the Curie temperature, spins align ferromagnetically; above it, thermal fluctuations disorder them. The partition function's behavior near the critical point reveals universal scaling laws: the correlation length diverges as |T − T_c|^{−ν}, the magnetization as |T − T_c|^β. These critical exponents are universal—independent of microscopic details—which is why the Ising model describes transitions in magnets, binary liquids, and protein folding.

Entropy and the Second Law

The Boltzmann entropy S = k_B ln Ω counts the number of accessible microstates Ω. The Second Law—entropy increases in isolated systems—follows from the overwhelming probability of high-entropy states: there are astronomically more disordered configurations than ordered ones. If 10²³ gas molecules are confined to the left half of a container and the partition is removed, expansion is certain not because of any force but because the fraction of microstates with all molecules on the left is ∼2^{−10²³}—effectively zero probability. Irreversibility emerges from probability, not from any violation of microscopic time-reversibility.

Quantum Statistical Mechanics

At low temperatures or high densities, quantum effects become important. Identical quantum particles obey either Fermi-Dirac statistics (fermions: electrons, protons—no two in the same state) or Bose-Einstein statistics (bosons: photons, helium-4—can share states). The Fermi-Dirac distribution explains metallic conductivity and the stability of white dwarf stars (electron degeneracy pressure). The Bose-Einstein distribution explains blackbody radiation (Planck's law) and predicts Bose-Einstein condensation—a phase transition where bosons macroscopically occupy the lowest energy state, observed experimentally in 1995 and underlying superconductivity and superfluidity.

Conclusion

Statistical mechanics is one of physics' greatest intellectual achievements: deriving thermodynamics from mechanics by embracing ignorance rather than fighting it. By treating the unknowable positions and velocities of 10²³ particles statistically, it derives temperature, pressure, heat capacity, and phase transitions from first principles. The Boltzmann distribution and partition function encode all equilibrium thermodynamics, while the entropy-as-microstate-counting resolves the arrow of time. Statistical mechanics reveals that macroscopic certainty is simply the overwhelming weight of probability at astronomical scales.
