The Law of Large Numbers: Why Casinos Always Win

#21 May 25, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

Flip a fair coin 10 times, and getting 7 heads wouldn't surprise you. Flip it 10,000 times, and getting 7,000 heads would be extraordinary. This intuition captures the Law of Large Numbers (LLN)—one of probability theory's foundational theorems, and the mathematical reason casinos profit reliably, insurance companies price accurately, and scientific experiments yield trustworthy results.

Weak and Strong Laws

The Weak Law: for any ε > 0, the probability that the sample mean deviates from the true mean by more than ε approaches zero as n → ∞:

P(|X̄ − μ| > ε) → 0 as n → ∞

The Strong Law makes an almost-certain statement: P(lim X̄ = μ as n → ∞) = 1. For practical purposes, both say the same thing: average enough, and you get the truth.

Why Casinos Win

Roulette has 38 slots: 18 red, 18 black, 2 green. A $1 bet on red has expected value:

(18/38)×(+1) + (20/38)×(−1) = −$0.053

You lose 5.3 cents per dollar bet on average. For a single bet, this is imperceptible. But the casino takes millions of bets. By the LLN, their average return converges to −5.3 cents for players. The house edge is a mathematical certainty at scale.

Insurance and Risk Pooling

Insurance companies profit from the same principle. The probability of any individual having a house fire is unpredictable—but across 100,000 policyholders, the fraction experiencing fires converges to the true probability. Risk pooling transforms individual uncertainty into collective predictability, making the business of insurance mathematically viable.

The Gambler's Fallacy

The LLN is frequently misunderstood as implying a 'correction mechanism'—that after a run of heads, tails become more likely. This is the Gambler's Fallacy. Coins have no memory. Convergence happens through dilution—thousands of future normal flips overwhelm the initial streak—not through compensating bad luck.

Conclusion

The Law of Large Numbers bridges probability theory and observed reality. It explains why randomness doesn't preclude reliability—why casinos profit, why insurance works, why science converges. Individual outcomes may be unpredictable, but averages are not. The LLN transforms probability from a theoretical framework into a practical tool.
