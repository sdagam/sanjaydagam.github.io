Graph Neural Networks: Deep Learning on Structured Data

#122 of 156 May 2, 2026 Group 5 · Jan–Jun 2026

Drug discovery typically takes 12 years and costs over $2 billion. Most of that time is spent testing molecules that turn out to be ineffective or toxic — expensive failures that could in principle be predicted before synthesis. A molecule is a graph: atoms are nodes, chemical bonds are edges. Graph Neural Networks learn to predict molecular properties directly from this graph structure, identifying promising drug candidates from billions of possibilities without synthesizing a single compound in the lab.

Why Standard Neural Networks Can't Handle Graphs

Standard neural networks expect inputs of a fixed size — 784 pixel values for a 28×28 image, or a fixed-length text embedding. Graphs come in all sizes: a water molecule has 3 atoms, a protein might have thousands. Graphs also lack a natural ordering — atom #1 and atom #2 is an arbitrary labeling, but a neural network treats position 1 and position 2 as fundamentally different inputs. You'd need the network to give the same answer regardless of how the atoms are numbered. Standard networks can't do this.

The Graph Neural Network Idea: Pass Messages Along Edges

A Graph Neural Network (GNN) processes graphs by having nodes exchange messages with their neighbors — a process called message passing. Each node starts with a feature vector describing it (atom type, charge, mass). At each layer, every node sends a message to each neighbor, receives messages from all its neighbors, and updates its own feature vector based on the received messages.

One GNN layer — update for node v: h_v^(new) = UPDATE(h_v^(old), AGGREGATE({h_u : u is neighbor of v})) h_v = feature vector of node v (describes this atom/entity) AGGREGATE = combine all neighbor messages (e.g., sum or average them) UPDATE = a neural network that combines old features with neighbor info After k layers, each node's features encode information from its k-hop neighborhood (atoms within k bonds away).

After several layers of message passing, each node's feature vector has absorbed information from its wider neighborhood. The carbon atom "knows" what it's bonded to and what those atoms are bonded to, several steps out. For molecular property prediction, after all message-passing layers, you combine (aggregate) all node features into a single graph-level feature vector and pass it through a final neural network to predict properties like toxicity, solubility, or drug efficacy.

Permutation Invariance: The Key Property

GNNs are permutation invariant: renumber the atoms in any order, and the model gives the same prediction. This is guaranteed by the message-passing design. Each node only cares about its neighbors, and the aggregation (sum or average) doesn't depend on their ordering. A molecule is a molecule regardless of how its atoms are labeled — the GNN respects this.

Drug Discovery in Practice

Companies like Relay Therapeutics and Insilico Medicine use GNNs to screen billions of molecular candidates computationally. Given a target protein involved in a disease, a GNN trained on known drug-protein binding data predicts which new molecules are likely to bind effectively — narrowing billions of candidates to thousands worth synthesizing and testing. This reduces early-stage drug discovery from years to months. In 2020, a GNN-designed antibiotic (halicin) was found to kill drug-resistant bacteria that conventional antibiotics couldn't touch — discovered from a computational screen of over 100 million molecules.

Other Applications

GNNs predict traffic flow in city networks (nodes are intersections, edges are roads), recommend content in social networks (nodes are users and items, edges are interactions), detect fraud in financial transaction graphs, analyze power grid vulnerabilities, and predict protein-protein interactions in biology. Any data that has a natural graph structure — and an enormous amount of important data does — is a candidate for GNN analysis.

Conclusion

Graph Neural Networks extend deep learning to structured data by having nodes exchange information with their neighbors iteratively. Each layer of message passing lets every node absorb information from a wider radius in the graph. Permutation invariance ensures the model respects the fact that graphs have no natural ordering. In drug discovery, this transforms the search for new medicines: instead of synthesizing and testing compounds one by one, GNNs screen billions computationally, identifying the most promising candidates before a single experiment is run.
