Epidemic Spreading: The Mathematics of Contagion

#22 June 1, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

In December 2019, a handful of people in Wuhan had a novel respiratory illness. Within months, it had spread to every country on Earth. Understanding how diseases spread—and how to stop them—requires mathematical models that capture both the biology of infection and the structure of human contact networks. The SIR model and its variants provide this framework.

The SIR Model

The simplest epidemic model divides the population into Susceptible (S), Infected (I), and Recovered (R). The dynamics:

dS/dt = −βSI/N
dI/dt = βSI/N − γI
dR/dt = γI

where β is transmission rate, γ is recovery rate, and N is total population. This system captures the epidemic dynamic: an initial exponential rise, a peak, and a decline as susceptibles are depleted.

The Basic Reproduction Number

The most important quantity in epidemiology:

R₀ = β/γ

R₀ is the average number of secondary infections one infected person causes in a fully susceptible population. If R₀ > 1, the epidemic grows. If R₀ < 1, the disease dies out. COVID-19 had R₀ around 2–3; measles around 12–18, requiring ~95% vaccination for herd immunity.

Herd Immunity Threshold

Herd immunity occurs when enough of the population is immune that the disease can't sustain itself. The threshold is 1 − 1/R₀. For COVID-19 (R₀ = 3), about 67%. For measles (R₀ = 15), about 93%. Achieving this through vaccination protects those who can't be vaccinated—the immunocompromised, infants, those with vaccine allergies.

Network Structure Matters

The SIR model assumes random mixing. Real contact networks have structure: clustering, hubs, communities. Scale-free networks have no epidemic threshold—even very weakly transmissible diseases can spread through highly connected hubs. Targeting hubs for vaccination is far more efficient than random vaccination.

Mathematical models evaluate interventions quantitatively. Reducing β through masks, increasing γ through treatment, and increasing immunity through vaccination all reduce the effective reproduction number R_eff = R₀ × S/N.

Conclusion

Epidemic models transform qualitative understanding of disease spread into quantitative predictions guiding public health decisions. The mathematics—differential equations, network theory, and probability—helps us understand thresholds, evaluate interventions, and anticipate future trajectories. COVID-19 brought these models to public attention, demonstrating both their power and their limitations when human behavior and viral evolution interact with mathematical predictions.
