Plain-English Summary

Dairy farming is extraordinarily complex — a farm's profitability depends on thousands of interacting decisions about herd management, feeding, waste handling, and climate. Prof. Victor Cabrera at UW–Madison has built mathematical models that help farmers and extension agents simulate and optimize those decisions.

This project has two tracks running in parallel. The first is documentation: scripting and producing professional tutorial videos for two live web tools so that dairy producers across the country can use them effectively. The second is engineering: diagnosing and repairing a powerful whole-farm simulation model that has been completely inaccessible on modern computers since 2016, due to a broken dependency on discontinued Microsoft software.

Tool 1 — AMS Transition Budgeter

Should a dairy farm switch to robotic milking?

Automatic Milking Systems (robots) represent one of the largest capital investments a dairy producer can make — typically $200,000 per robot plus $50,000 in retrofit costs. The AMS Transition Budgeter, built by Prof. Cabrera's lab and available at dairymgt.cals.wisc.edu, uses a partial-budget approach combined with multi-year cash flow projections to answer not just whether the investment pays off, but when.

Key Inputs and Outputs

Web tool · Live
Farm Baseline

Herd size, milk yield (lbs/cow/day), milk price ($/cwt), daily milking labor hours, labor rate ($/hr). Defaults: 120 cows, 80 lbs, $18/cwt, 12 hrs/day, $20/hr.

Investment & Specs

Number of robots, cost per robot, retrofit cost, loan rate, loan term, labor per robot per day. Default: 2 robots at $200k each, $50k retrofit, 7.5%, 10 years.

Operational Impacts

Milk yield change (default +5% from increased visit frequency), feed cost adjustment ($0.15/cow/day for robot pellet), annual maintenance per robot ($8,000).

Key Outputs

Net Annual Impact, Labor Saved (hours + FTE equivalent), Breakeven Wage, 15-year Cash Flow Timeline, Breakeven Gauge, and Sensitivity Tornado chart.

The Breakeven Wage insight

The tool's most decision-relevant output is the Breakeven Wage — the minimum hourly labor rate at which the robot investment has net present value of zero. If a farm's actual labor cost exceeds this threshold, the investment is economically favorable. The sensitivity analysis shows that milk price and labor rate are typically the two highest-leverage variables.

Sanjay scripted and produced the full tutorial video for this tool — writing the walkthrough script, recording screen capture of the live tool, generating AI voiceover, and editing the final MP4. The video has been posted to YouTube and linked on the lab's tools page.

Tool 2 — Heat Abatement Investment Scouter

Does cooling equipment pay for itself?

High-producing dairy cows begin experiencing heat stress when the Temperature-Humidity Index (THI) exceeds 68 — a threshold routinely exceeded across much of the U.S. for months at a time. The Heat Abatement Investment Scouter uses 10-year average climate data for any location in the country to estimate milk losses from heat stress and calculate whether an investment in fans and soakers delivers a positive return.

Methodology and Key Outputs

Web tool · Live
THI Calculation

Uses location-specific 10-year average climate data (temp, humidity) to compute monthly THI values and classify heat stress hours as mild (68–72), moderate (72–78), or severe (78+).

Milk Loss Model

Estimates milk production loss at 0.5 lbs per cow per hour per THI unit above threshold (Mader et al. 2006). Applies farm-specific herd size and yield inputs.

Investment Analysis

Fan + soaker + installation capital costs, loan terms, operating costs (electricity + water), and cooling effectiveness (default 65%). Computes payback period, net annual gain, and 5-year ROI.

2030 Projection

Includes a forward-looking climate risk projection showing expected heat stress hours in 2030, helping producers factor in a warming baseline when evaluating long-term investments.

Sanjay wrote the script, produced the video, and reshot it after the tool was updated — revising the script to match the new THI methodology and recording a final version. The video is published on YouTube and linked on the lab's tools page.

Tool 3 — DyNoFlo

A whole-farm dairy simulation — and an engineering problem

DyNoFlo (Dynamic North Florida Dairy Farm Model) is Prof. Cabrera's most comprehensive tool: an integrated Excel-based simulation combining six distinct modeling components to answer a single central question — can a dairy farm reduce nitrogen leaching while maintaining profitability, and how should management strategies change based on climate forecasts?

Six Integrated Model Components

Markov + DSSAT + LP
Markov-Chain Herd Model

3,200 cow states defined by months in milk, pregnancy status, and lactation number. Computes monthly milk production, dry matter intake, and manure nitrogen excretion per animal group.

DSSAT Crop System

Pre-computed crop outputs for up to 10 fields with configurable forage rotations (bermudagrass, corn, sorghum) across seasons. Results stratified by soil type and ENSO phase.

Nitrogen Tracking

Follows manure N through the entire waste chain — flushing, pond, sludge, spraying, soil application — with configurable loss rates. Cumulative N lost shown as a percentage.

ENSO Climate Module

Assigns El Niño, La Niña, Neutral, or 43-year average to each simulation year. El Niño winters drive the most leaching; January alone accounts for 30–40% of annual nitrogen loss.

Economic Analysis

Monthly and annual profit from milk, cow/heifer sales, and expense categories. Case study result: optimizer recommended changes reduced leaching 25% while increasing profit 3%.

LP Optimizer

Maximizes profit subject to a nitrogen constraint (or vice versa), solved independently per ENSO phase. Outputs climate-specific management strategies for crude protein, forage rotations, and confined time.

The compatibility problem

DyNoFlo's simulation engine, data, formulas, charts, and dashboard are fully intact. But the entire VBA layer — all macros, all modules, all interactive buttons — is inaccessible on any Excel version released after 2003. This means the model has been effectively unusable for over two decades on modern machines.

Two compounding failures
1
OWC10 / 64-bit incompatibility. The VBA project references Microsoft Office XP Web Components (OWC10), discontinued after Office 2003. On 64-bit Excel, VBA API declarations also require the PtrSafe keyword — absent throughout the original code. Together these cause a compile failure that blocks everything: no macros run, the VBA editor won't load, and Excel refuses to save any changes. A catch-22 where the broken state prevents the fix.
2
Missing linked workbooks. DyNoFlo depends on external companion files containing pre-computed DSSAT crop model tables. Without them at the expected file paths, the simulation runs for approximately 3 years then halts with "Couldn't get updated values from linked workbook."

Three repair pathways

01 Install OWC10 on 32-bit Excel. Download the free Microsoft Office XP Web Components redistributable, install it to register the missing type library, and re-attempt reference cleanup. Only viable on 32-bit Excel installations.
02 Fix on older Office machine. Open on Office 2003/2007 where OWC10 is available. Uncheck the four broken references, remove the nine OWC-dependent UserForms, import a replacement macro module (RunModel.bas), and save. Test on modern Excel.
03 Full rebuild. Open with macros disabled, copy all sheet data into a new .xlsm workbook, re-import 11 VBA module files from the extracted rebuild kit, add macro buttons, and re-embed the DSSAT companion data as local sheets to eliminate external links. Most effort — most portable result.

The simulation engine itself (the MARKOV subroutine in Module1) requires no logic changes — it reads entirely from worksheet cells with no dependency on OWC or UserForms. Once the VBA layer is accessible, the fix is straightforward. Sanjay is currently pursuing access to the VBA project password from Prof. Cabrera to enable Option 02 or 03.

Key Insights from DyNoFlo Research

What the model reveals about dairy nitrogen management

  • El Niño winters drive the most nitrogen leaching — wetter, cooler conditions increase water percolation and reduce plant nitrogen uptake. La Niña years show the opposite.
  • January alone accounts for 30–40% of total annual nitrogen leaching in the case study farm, making winter management the highest-leverage intervention window.
  • Sandy, shallow soils leach dramatically more nitrogen than more retentive soils under identical management — soil type selection and site matching are critical.
  • The most effective levers for reducing leaching without sacrificing profit: lowering dietary crude protein, planting bermudagrass in pastures and sprayfields, and strip-planting corn into bermudagrass sod.
  • Climate-adaptive management matters: optimizer recommendations differ by ENSO phase. When El Niño is forecast, additional strategies include voluntary culling, exporting manure off-farm, or renting additional pasture.
  • In the published 400-cow case study, optimization reduced nitrogen leaching by 25% while increasing profit by 3%. Feasible adjustments (a practical subset) still achieved 23% leaching reduction with 2.5% profit gain.
