Study of Ozone Exceedance Days in Wisconsin
Due to Canadian Wildfires

A 16-year analysis of 2,418 ozone-season days across 39 Wisconsin monitoring sites — revealing that temperature and smoke interact synergistically to drive exceedances, and that lakeshore communities face 3× higher risk than inland areas.

Plain-English Summary

When Canadian wildfires send smoke drifting into Wisconsin, ozone levels spike — sometimes crossing the federal safety limit. But 2023 had 39 exceedance days while 2025 had only 5, even though both years had massive wildfires. Why? This study found the answer: it's not just the smoke — it's the heat. Temperature is the dominant driver of ozone exceedances, and smoke amplifies it. On a smoky day, Wisconsin can hit dangerous ozone levels at 83°F — a temperature that would be perfectly safe on a clear day. The lakeshore is especially vulnerable.

The Central Puzzle

Two massive wildfire years — vastly different outcomes

Both 2023 and 2025 saw record-setting Canadian wildfires with comparable PM2.5 levels, yet their ozone impacts were dramatically different. A simple year-to-year comparison wasn't revealing, so the analysis was extended to 2010–2025 to identify the underlying drivers.

Data & Methods

A 16-year, multi-source dataset

The study merged five independent data sources into a single 2,418-day dataset covering the Wisconsin ozone season (May–September) from 2010 to 2025, with zero missing values across all predictors.

  • Daily max ozone: EPA AQS Parameter 44201, max across ~31 Wisconsin monitors per day
  • Daily max PM2.5: EPA AQS, max across 25 Wisconsin monitors (151,367 total records)
  • Daily max temperature: IEM ASOS, max across 5 Wisconsin stations (Sheboygan, Milwaukee, Green Bay, Madison, Appleton)
  • Fire proximity: Canadian National Fire Database (2010–2024) and NASA FIRMS VIIRS (2025), fires ≥200 ha within 1,500 km
  • Site-level validation: 54,197 site-days across 39 ozone monitors, each paired with nearest PM2.5 station (median distance: 9.6 km)

Analysis used Python 3.12 with NumPy, SciPy, and Pandas. Linear regression via Ordinary Least Squares; logistic regression via Newton-Raphson maximum likelihood estimation (convergence criterion: max|Δβ| < 10⁻⁸).

Linear Regression Results

How much does each factor add to ozone?

The best-fit linear model uses temperature and PM2.5 together, explaining 35.7% of daily ozone variance across all 2,418 days.

Key insight — fire proximity doesn't matter

Fire proximity alone (just having fires within 1,500 km) explains only 2.1% of variance. It adds nothing once PM2.5 is in the model. What matters is whether smoke actually arrives and delivers particulates — not whether fires exist upwind.

The interaction term (Temp × PM2.5) is highly significant (p = 1.2 × 10⁻¹²), confirming that smoke has a larger effect on hot days than cool days. On a 90°F day, each µg/m³ of PM2.5 drives more ozone than on a 70°F day — a synergistic, not merely additive, relationship.

Logistic Regression Results

Predicting whether exceedance will occur

The logistic model answers the more practical question: on a given day, how likely is an ozone exceedance? The model achieves AUC = 0.885 — excellent discrimination.

Logistic model (AUC = 0.885, n = 2,418) P(exc) = 1 / (1 + e^(23.50 − 0.247×Temp − 0.041×PM2.5)) Each 1°F → odds × 1.28  |  Each 10°F → odds × 11.8×  |  Each 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 → odds × 1.5×  |  Smoke Likely (binary) → odds × 2.8×
The 16°F smoke threshold shift

Smoke shifts the exceedance probability curve leftward by approximately 7°F. On a smoky day, conditions at 83°F carry the same exceedance risk as clean air at 90°F. On clean-air days, the EPA threshold (70 ppb) isn't crossed until temperatures reach approximately 104°F.

Temperature alone achieves AUC = 0.872 — already excellent. Adding PM2.5 improves it to 0.885. The Smoke Likely binary variable (fire within 1,500 km AND PM2.5 > 15 µg/m³) multiplies exceedance odds by 2.8× after controlling for temperature (p < 0.001).

Lakeshore vs Inland

The lakefront is a distinct, high-risk zone

Site-level analysis (54,197 site-days across 39 monitors) revealed a stark geographic divide. Lakefront sites along Lake Michigan show 3× higher exceedance rates and respond more strongly to both heat and smoke.

This is consistent with the lake-breeze recirculation mechanism: onshore winds trap ozone precursors in a shallow boundary layer near the shoreline, amplifying the effects of both heat and smoke. On smoky days (PM2.5 ≥ 15), lakefront sites gain +14.9 ppb versus +9.1 ppb inland.

A significant south-to-north gradient exists along Lake Michigan. Mean ozone drops 2.1 ppb per degree of latitude (r = −0.85, p = 0.002), reflecting proximity to Chicago/Milwaukee urban precursor emissions that make heat+smoke chemistry more potent at southern sites. Two sites stand out:

Key Findings

What the data shows

  • Temperature is the dominant driver of ozone exceedances, explaining 32.7% of variance alone — strong for a single environmental variable.
  • Smoke acts as a powerful amplifier: on Smoke Likely days, exceedance odds are 2.8× higher after controlling for temperature (4.1× at the site level).
  • Fire proximity alone is not meaningful — only confirmed smoke delivery (fire + PM2.5 > 15 µg/m³) carries predictive power.
  • The critical danger zone for ozone is moderate smoke (PM2.5 15–25 µg/m³), not extreme smoke — very heavy smoke may actually block UV radiation needed for ozone photochemistry.
  • 2025's low exceedance count despite heavy wildfires is fully explained by fewer hot days — the model correctly predicts this outcome.
  • Lakefront sites show a 3× higher exceedance rate and 50% stronger temperature sensitivity than inland sites.
  • A statistically significant south-to-north decline in ozone risk exists along Lake Michigan (r = −0.85, p = 0.002).
Comparison to Literature

Regional specificity beyond existing work

Lee and Jaffe (2024, Environmental Science and Technology) examined 600 ozone monitoring sites across the U.S., studying environmental fires and PM2.5 using a generalized additive model with more variables (R² = 0.6).

This study's statewide model achieves R² = 0.357 with only two predictors, while site-level analysis reaches R² = 0.604 at Racine. The added value here is regional specificity: the lakeshore vs. inland heterogeneity, the south-to-north gradient, and the interaction dynamics unique to Wisconsin's geography are not captured in national-scale analyses.

A next step is to adopt the Lee and Jaffe protocol — incorporating additional variables and a generalized additive model — to improve statewide predictive power while retaining the Wisconsin-specific geographic analysis.

Real-World Applications

From analysis to action

The logistic model provides a practical decision tool: given a forecast temperature and PM2.5 level, public health agencies can estimate same-day exceedance probability with AUC = 0.885 accuracy. Lakeshore communities — especially in Racine, Kenosha, and Milwaukee — warrant heightened alert protocols on days combining heat forecasts with active wildfire smoke advisories.
