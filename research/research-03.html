Wisconsin Precipitation is Redistributing —
Fewer Light Events, More Intense Rainfall

An 80-year analysis of daily precipitation at six Wisconsin First Order Stations, uncovering a statistically significant shift in rainfall intensity — and a 2–2.5× amplification of moderate rain growth at Great Lakes shoreline stations compared to inland sites.

Supervisor Dr. Paul Buchmann, WI State Climatology Office
Institution University of Wisconsin–Madison
Timeline December 2025 – Present
Status Active
~80 Years of data (1948–2025)
6 First Order Stations
5 Precipitation categories

Plain-English Summary

Wisconsin's rainfall is changing in a way that matters for infrastructure, agriculture, and flood planning. It's not just getting wetter — the character of precipitation is shifting. Light, misty days are disappearing while moderate and heavy rain events are becoming more common. And this shift is especially pronounced near the Great Lakes shoreline, where Milwaukee and Green Bay are seeing the effect at more than twice the rate of inland cities like Wausau and Eau Claire.

Research Scope

Three parallel analyses of Wisconsin's changing climate

In collaboration with Dr. Buchmann at the Wisconsin State Climatology Office, this project investigates three related questions about how Wisconsin's winter and precipitation climate is changing, using daily records from the state's six First Order Stations going back to roughly 1948.

🌧 Precipitation Distribution

How is the distribution of daily rainfall shifting across intensity categories? Are heavy events becoming more frequent while light events decline?

Results in hand
🌡 Freeze-Thaw Cycles

How often does daily average temperature cross 32°F in each winter? Infrastructure and plant roots are damaged by repeated freezing and thawing cycles.

In progress
☀️ Melting Degree Days

How many cumulative degrees above freezing does each winter accumulate? This tracks snowpack persistence — critical for ski tourism and spring frost risk.

Data & Methods

~80 years of daily records at six First Order Stations

Daily precipitation data were obtained from the Iowa Environmental Mesonet (Iowa State University), which archives ASOS station records for all U.S. states. The six Wisconsin First Order Stations — the airports that maintain the longest, most reliable continuous records — were selected as the core dataset.

Each day was classified into one of five precipitation categories: dry (0"), light (0–0.5"), moderate (0.5–1"), heavy (1–2"), and extreme (2+"). Days were expressed as a percentage of annual days in each category, and trends were measured in percent per year. Missing data days were excluded from analysis. Duluth (DLH) was analyzed but excluded from primary results due to a data inconsistency — trace precipitation was recorded as exactly 0.00" until approximately 2000, creating a structural break in the series.

Two independent statistical methods were applied to each trend: Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) linear regression for slope and R², and the Mann-Kendall test for non-parametric trend significance. Agreement between the two methods strengthens confidence in reported findings.

Station-Level Results

Six stations, two geographic profiles

The same analysis was run at all six First Order Stations to test whether Milwaukee's pattern was a statewide phenomenon or geographically specific. The most striking finding: the shift toward moderate precipitation (0.5–1") is 2–2.5× stronger at the two lakeside stations than at inland sites.

The Lakeside Effect

Great Lakes proximity amplifies the redistribution

The most geographically interesting finding is the difference between lakeside and inland stations in the moderate precipitation category (0.5–1"). Milwaukee and Green Bay — both on or near Lake Michigan — show significant upward trends in this category at rates roughly 2–2.5× those of Wausau and Eau Claire inland.

Lakeside vs. inland comparison — moderate rain (0.5–1")

Milwaukee: +0.016%/yr (p<0.001) · Green Bay: +0.011%/yr (p<0.05) vs. Wausau: +0.006%/yr (not significant) · Eau Claire: +0.003%/yr (not significant). The lakeside trend is statistically significant; the inland trend is not. This pattern is consistent with lake-breeze recirculation mechanisms concentrating moisture and precipitation near the shoreline — but the specific magnitude and intensity-category specificity of this effect in Wisconsin is not well documented in existing literature.

La Crosse shows a different profile: strong growth in the heavy category (1–2", +0.016%/yr, p<0.001) with no significant change in moderate rain. This may reflect its position along the Mississippi River valley, which creates its own mesoscale moisture dynamics independent of the Great Lakes.

Surprising Finding

The light rain decline is almost entirely trace events

The 0–0.5" light rain category is declining strongly at most stations. A sub-analysis of Milwaukee's light rain days revealed that this decline is not spread evenly across the category — it is almost entirely driven by trace precipitation events (≤0.01"), the kind of fine mist or drizzle that barely registers.

Milwaukee sub-analysis — breakdown of 0–0.5" category
Trace ≤0.01" −6.6% total change · p<0.0001 · DRIVING THE DECLINE
0.01–0.10" −1.1% total change · p=0.159 · not significant
0.10–0.25" −0.4% total change · p=0.503 · not significant
0.25–0.50" +0.5% total change · p=0.250 · not significant

This means the redistribution story is more nuanced than "light rain is declining." What's actually happening is that the very lightest precipitation events — mist, drizzle, trace rainfall — are disappearing, while light rain proper (0.01–0.5") is largely stable. This sub-analysis has so far only been run for Milwaukee; whether it holds at the other five stations is an open question.

Key Findings

What the data shows across all six stations

  • Wisconsin precipitation is redistributing toward greater intensity: dry days and light rain are increasing and decreasing respectively, while moderate and heavy events are growing.
  • Total annual precipitation has increased alongside this shift — the state is not just experiencing redistribution but also cumulative growth.
  • The moderate rain increase (0.5–1") is 2–2.5× stronger at Great Lakes shoreline stations (Milwaukee, Green Bay) than at inland stations (Wausau, Eau Claire), where the trend is not statistically significant.
  • The decline in light precipitation at Milwaukee is almost entirely driven by disappearing trace events (≤0.01"), not a reduction in ordinary light rain.
  • Both OLS linear regression and Mann-Kendall non-parametric tests agree on the direction and significance of all reported trends, strengthening the findings.
  • La Crosse is an outlier: unlike the other five stations, its strongest growth is in heavy precipitation (1–2"), not moderate rain, possibly due to Mississippi River valley moisture dynamics.
Next Steps

Expanding the analysis — confirmed with Dr. Buchmann

Dr. Buchmann confirmed in March 2026 that the findings warrant expansion. The following next steps were agreed upon:

01 Convert results from %/year to days/year for easier interpretation, and re-express all trends accordingly.
02 Run the trace precipitation sub-analysis (breakdown of 0–0.5") at all six stations to determine whether Milwaukee's result is representative statewide.
03 Break the analysis into seasons — fall and winter are especially interesting for testing the lake effect precipitation hypothesis.
04 Expand to 20–30 stations around the Great Lakes region (including Illinois, Michigan, and Minnesota) to formally test whether the lakeside amplification holds at larger geographic scale.
05 Develop a spatial map of trends across all stations — Dr. Buchmann is researching display methods for showing trend variability on a geographic map.
06 When enough lakeside and inland stations are assembled, combine groups for box-and-whisker comparison plots and a difference-in-means test.
