Project 05 Machine Learning Remote Sensing Air Quality

Ground-Level NO₂ Predictions Using
Satellite Imagery-Driven Convolutional Neural Networks

Training a CNN on both TROPOMI and TEMPO satellite data to predict surface NO₂ concentrations across Wisconsin — filling the gap left by sparse ground monitors and testing whether TEMPO's hourly imagery can improve spatial generalization.

Plain-English Summary

Wisconsin has only three NO₂ air quality monitors, all located in Milwaukee. For everyone else in the state — from Green Bay to rural farm communities — there is no direct measurement of this harmful pollutant. Satellites can see the whole state, but satellite readings don't directly translate to ground-level concentrations.

This project is building a convolutional neural network — the same type of AI used in image recognition — to read satellite imagery and predict what the ground-level NO₂ would be at any location in Wisconsin. The key novelty: training the model on NASA's new TEMPO satellite, which captures hourly NO₂ data at higher resolution than any prior satellite, alongside the established TROPOMI instrument. No published model has been trained on TEMPO data yet.

The Research Gap

Three open problems this project addresses

Identified gaps in the literature
1

No TEMPO-trained models exist. CNN and regression models have been trained on OMI and TROPOMI satellite data. TEMPO launched in April 2023 and provides hourly NO₂ at 2.1 km × 4.4 km resolution over North America — higher temporal and spatial resolution than any prior instrument. No published model has used it as a training input.

2

Spatial generalization is the field's central unsolved problem. Cao (2023) achieved R² = 0.892 with standard cross-validation but only R² = 0.593 when predicting at sites never seen during training. Kim et al. (2024) similarly found their best model generalized only to R² = 0.65–0.89 regionally. TEMPO's hourly data may enable the model to learn more robust spatial features.

3

Wisconsin is severely underserved. The entire state of Wisconsin — 65,000 square miles with agricultural, urban, and industrial zones — is monitored by only three NO₂ sensors, all concentrated in Milwaukee. Environmental equity concerns require better spatial coverage, especially in communities of color and low-income areas that may be disproportionately exposed.

Scientific Background

Why predicting surface NO₂ from satellites is hard

NO₂ is a harmful air pollutant produced primarily by fossil fuel combustion. It causes cardiovascular and respiratory illness, contributes to childhood asthma, and reacts in the atmosphere to form tropospheric ozone and PM2.5. The EPA requires annual mean concentrations to stay below 53 ppb in inhabited areas.

The problem is that satellites don't measure ground-level NO₂ — they measure the total column of NO₂ through the entire atmosphere, with greater sensitivity to NO₂ in the middle troposphere than at the surface. The relationship between column NO₂ (what satellites see) and surface NO₂ (what humans breathe) varies by location, land use, season, road proximity, and meteorological conditions. This gap is what machine learning models are designed to bridge.

Because NO₂ has a short atmospheric lifetime of only a few hours, it remains concentrated near its source. This produces sharp spatial gradients — NO₂ can vary by a factor of 10 across a few kilometers — that simple regression models cannot capture from coarse buffer distance averages. CNNs, which learn spatial patterns directly from 2D satellite imagery, are better suited to this problem.

Kim et al.'s regression approach is interpretable and requires minimal computation — but it cannot capture the spatial structure of satellite data. Cao's CNN achieves far higher accuracy but fails at the key real-world challenge of predicting in locations it hasn't seen during training. The research question here is whether TEMPO data can close that gap.

Proposed Approach

Following Cao's CNN protocol, extended with TEMPO

The core methodology follows Cao (2023): a convolutional neural network that receives stacked 2D satellite imagery grids around each EPA monitoring site and predicts surface NO₂ concentration. The key extension is training on both TROPOMI and TEMPO inputs, plus adapting the pipeline specifically for Wisconsin's monitoring context.

The central hypothesis is that TEMPO's hourly temporal resolution allows the model to observe how NO₂ changes throughout the day at thousands of locations — providing a richer signal about local emission sources, boundary layer dynamics, and photochemistry that TROPOMI's single daily overpass cannot capture. This richer training signal may help the model generalize to locations it hasn't seen, addressing the field's most persistent limitation.

Research Plan

Five phases from data to Wisconsin maps

01
Problem Definition and Data Inventory

Define target variable (daily / monthly surface NO₂ in ppb), identify training inputs (TROPOMI, TEMPO, ERA5 meteorology, land use, road networks, population density), and establish spatial and temporal resolution. Inventory Wisconsin's 3 EPA monitors and assess CONUS monitor coverage for training data.

02
Data Acquisition and Pipeline

Extract TROPOMI and TEMPO NO₂ from Google Earth Engine (GEE) as 2D pixel grids. Download ERA5 meteorological reanalysis (temperature, wind, boundary layer height, pressure, radiation). Acquire land-use variables (NLCD impervious surface, MODIS vegetation, population density, road networks from OpenStreetMap). Align all sources spatially and temporally to EPA monitor locations.

03
Preprocessing and Feature Engineering

Handle satellite data gaps from cloud cover. Apply Anscombe transform to TROPOMI NO₂ (following Kim et al. 2024 best practice) and test appropriate transforms for TEMPO. Construct feature grids at multiple resolutions (500m–4,000m). Engineer temporal features: sine/cosine encodings of hour and month, lag variables, boundary layer ratio features.

04
Model Training and Evaluation

Train CNN architecture following Cao (2023) — Architecture 1 (stacked multi-channel tensor, four 2D convolution layers). Benchmark against MLR, FNN, and Random Forest baselines using identical variables. Evaluate with standard 10-fold CV, spatial hold-out CV (unseen sites), and temporal hold-out CV. Target metrics: R², RMSE, MAE, and spatial CV R² (the critical generalization test).

05
Wisconsin NO₂ Mapping

Apply the trained model to generate statewide Wisconsin NO₂ concentration maps at 0.01° × 0.01° (≈1 km) resolution. Identify high-exposure zones not captured by existing monitors. Conduct SHAP analysis to interpret what spatial features the model learned. Compare lakeshore vs. inland patterns, urban vs. agricultural zones, and seasonal variation.

Research Questions

What we're asking — and why it matters

  • Does training on TEMPO hourly imagery improve spatial generalization compared to TROPOMI-only models — specifically, does spatial CV R² improve beyond Cao's 0.593 baseline?
  • Can the CNN produce reliable NO₂ estimates for locations in Wisconsin that have no EPA monitor, using TEMPO and TROPOMI data jointly?
  • What spatial features drive NO₂ predictions in Wisconsin's distinctive geography — lakeshore industrial corridors, agricultural zones, urban core, and rural areas?
  • Do communities without monitoring coverage show systematically higher or lower predicted NO₂ than monitored areas — and what are the environmental equity implications?
Literature Foundation

Key papers informing this work

Kim et al. (2024) — JGR Atmospheres Comparison of regression methods for inferring near-surface NO₂ with satellite data. Supervisor Prof. Holloway's lab at UW-Madison. Best result: Anscombe-transformed MLR, R² = 0.78 across CONUS. Motivates the data transform approach and establishes the regression baseline this project improves upon.
Cao (2023) — Frontiers in Environmental Science National ground-level NO₂ predictions via satellite imagery-driven CNNs. Fairview High School student. R² = 0.892 daily, R² = 0.952 annual — state of the art. Critical limitation: spatial CV R² = 0.593. This project follows Cao's protocol and tests whether TEMPO input addresses the spatial generalization failure.
Ghahremanloo et al. (2021) — JGR Atmospheres Deep learning estimation of daily ground-level NO₂ using TROPOMI over Texas. CNN outperformed MLR (R = 0.91 vs 0.77). First major demonstration of CNN superiority for this problem; established the deep learning paradigm this project builds on.
Zoogman et al. (2017) — JQSRT (TEMPO) Tropospheric Emissions: Monitoring of Pollution (TEMPO). Launch: April 2023. Hourly NO₂ at 2.1 km × 4.4 km over North America. First geostationary air quality satellite. This instrument provides the novel training data that no prior NO₂ CNN model has used.
