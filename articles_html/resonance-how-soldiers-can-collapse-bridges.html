Resonance: How Soldiers Can Collapse Bridges

February 24, 2024

In 1831, a regiment of British soldiers marching in step across the Broughton Suspension Bridge near Manchester caused it to collapse. In 1940, the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in Washington State oscillated wildly in moderate winds and dramatically fell apart—captured in one of engineering history's most famous films. Both disasters share a common cause: resonance, the phenomenon where a system is driven at its natural frequency, causing oscillations to grow dramatically. Understanding resonance is essential for engineers designing bridges, buildings, aircraft, and electronics.

Natural Frequency

Every physical system that can oscillate has a natural frequency—the rate at which it oscillates when disturbed and left alone. For a simple pendulum: f = (1/2pi) * sqrt(g/L), where g is gravitational acceleration and L is the pendulum's length. A 1-meter pendulum has a natural frequency of about 0.5 Hz. For a mass on a spring: f = (1/2pi) * sqrt(k/m), where k is the spring constant. Every bridge, building, wine glass, and tuning fork has its own natural frequency determined by its physical properties.

The Mathematics of Resonance

When an oscillating system is driven by an external force at frequency omega, the response amplitude depends critically on how close omega is to the natural frequency omega_0. For a simple harmonic oscillator with light damping: A(omega) = F_0/m / sqrt((omega_0^2 - omega^2)^2 + (2*zeta*omega_0*omega)^2), where F_0 is the driving force amplitude and zeta is the damping ratio. When omega = omega_0 (driving frequency equals natural frequency), the denominator is minimized and amplitude is maximized. With no damping (zeta = 0), the amplitude becomes infinite—resonance causes unbounded oscillation growth.

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge failure is often cited as a resonance disaster, but the physics is more subtle. The bridge didn't fail due to simple forced resonance from wind at the natural frequency. Instead, it suffered aeroelastic flutter—a feedback interaction between the bridge's motion and aerodynamic forces that reduced the effective damping to zero, allowing oscillations to grow. As the bridge twisted, the changed airflow angle altered the forces in a way that amplified rather than damped the motion. This self-exciting oscillation is qualitatively similar to resonance but distinct mechanically.

Constructive Uses

Resonance is not only destructive—it's essential in countless technologies. Musical instruments exploit resonance: a guitar string's resonant frequencies determine the notes it produces, and the guitar body resonates to amplify sound. MRI machines use nuclear magnetic resonance—hydrogen nuclei in the body resonate at specific radio frequencies in strong magnetic fields, enabling detailed tissue imaging. Lasers amplify light through stimulated emission at specific resonant frequencies. Radio and television tuners select specific frequencies by adjusting resonant circuits.

Avoiding Destructive Resonance

Modern engineering carefully avoids resonance in structural design. Bridges are designed so wind-induced forcing frequencies don't match the bridge's natural frequencies. Damping mechanisms—like the 730-ton steel pendulum inside Taipei 101 skyscraper—absorb energy and prevent resonant buildup during earthquakes and high winds. Car suspensions are tuned to avoid resonance with road frequencies. Precision instruments are isolated from building vibrations using anti-vibration tables with carefully chosen resonant properties.

Conclusion

Resonance demonstrates that the right frequency matters as much as the magnitude of a force. A gentle push at exactly the right moment—at the system's natural frequency—can build to catastrophic amplitude, while a much larger force at the wrong frequency does little. This principle applies from the microscopic (atomic absorption of light at specific frequencies) to the macroscopic (bridge oscillations, earthquake damage to buildings). Engineering's hard-won lesson from disasters like Tacoma Narrows is to never design a system without considering what happens when it's driven near its natural frequency.
