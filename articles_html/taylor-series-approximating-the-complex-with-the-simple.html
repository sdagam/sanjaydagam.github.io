Taylor Series: Approximating the Complex with the Simple

June 15, 2024

How does your calculator compute sin(0.3) or e^2.5? These functions aren't directly computable—they're defined by their mathematical properties, not by an algorithm. The answer involves Taylor series: representing any sufficiently smooth function as an infinite sum of polynomial terms. Polynomials are easy to compute—just multiplications and additions. Taylor series convert complex functions into polynomial approximations accurate to any desired precision, forming the computational backbone of scientific computing, physics, and engineering.

The Big Idea

A Taylor series expands a function f(x) around a point a as: f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + f''(a)(x-a)^2/2! + f'''(a)(x-a)^3/3! + ... Each term adds a correction that captures another aspect of the function's behavior near a. The zeroth-order approximation is just f(a)—a constant. Adding the first derivative term gives a line. Adding the second gives a parabola. More terms yield increasingly accurate approximations over a wider range.

Famous Examples

Some Taylor series are famous enough to memorize. Around x = 0 (called Maclaurin series): e^x = 1 + x + x^2/2! + x^3/3! + ... sin(x) = x - x^3/3! + x^5/5! - ... cos(x) = 1 - x^2/2! + x^4/4! - ... 1/(1-x) = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + ... These four series handle most functions encountered in physics and engineering.

Euler's Formula

Taylor series reveal one of mathematics' most stunning identities. Substituting ix into the exponential series: e^(ix) = cos(x) + i*sin(x). This is Euler's formula. Setting x = pi: e^(i*pi) + 1 = 0, combining e, i, pi, 1, and 0 in a single equation. Taylor series make this connection transparent—they reveal that exponentials and trigonometric functions are the same function, just evaluated at different arguments.

Error and Convergence

A Taylor series truncated after n terms is a polynomial approximation with an error bounded by Taylor's remainder theorem. The error depends on the magnitude of the (n+1)th derivative and the distance from the expansion point. For sin(x) near x = 0, the first three terms give an error less than 0.00003 for |x| < 1. This quantitative error control is what makes Taylor approximations useful.

Physics Applications

Taylor series are indispensable in physics. For small angles, sin(theta) approximately equals theta—the first Taylor term—simplifying pendulum analysis. For speeds much less than light, special relativity's energy formula expands to recover Newtonian kinetic energy as the leading correction. Quantum mechanics uses perturbation theory, essentially a Taylor expansion in the strength of a small interaction.

Conclusion

Taylor series reveal that smooth functions are, in a precise sense, made of polynomials. By expanding in derivatives, they expose a function's local structure and enable efficient computation. From calculator chips to quantum physics, Taylor series provide the bridge between abstract mathematical functions and concrete numerical computations—turning the complex into the polynomial, one derivative at a time.
