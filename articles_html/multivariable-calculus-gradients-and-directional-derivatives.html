Multivariable Calculus: Gradients and Directional Derivatives

September 28, 2024

Temperature varies across a room—hotter near the radiator, cooler by the window. The elevation of a landscape changes in every direction. The profit of a company depends on price, quantity, and a dozen other variables simultaneously. Multivariable calculus extends the tools of single-variable calculus to functions of many variables. The gradient and directional derivative are the central concepts, capturing how functions change in space and pointing the way toward maximum rates of change.

Partial Derivatives

For a function f(x, y), the partial derivative df/dx measures the rate of change of f with respect to x while holding y constant. Computing partial derivatives uses all the usual single-variable rules, treating the other variable as a constant. For f(x,y) = x^2*y + 3xy^2: df/dx = 2xy + 3y^2, df/dy = x^2 + 6xy.

The Gradient

The gradient of f, written grad(f) or nabla(f), collects all partial derivatives into a vector: grad(f) = (df/dx, df/dy, df/dz). This vector field has two crucial properties. First, it points in the direction of steepest ascent. Second, its magnitude |grad(f)| equals the maximum rate of change—the steepest slope at that point. Contour lines (where f is constant) are always perpendicular to the gradient.

Directional Derivatives

The directional derivative D_u f measures the rate of change of f in an arbitrary direction u (a unit vector): D_u f = grad(f) . u (dot product). The directional derivative is maximized when u aligns with grad(f)—confirming the gradient points in the steepest direction. It equals zero when u is perpendicular to grad(f)—moving along a contour line leaves f unchanged.

The Chain Rule in Multiple Dimensions

The multivariable chain rule handles composite functions. If z = f(x,y) where x = g(t) and y = h(t), then dz/dt = (df/dx)*(dx/dt) + (df/dy)*(dy/dt). In matrix notation: if f: R^n -> R^m, the derivative is a Jacobian matrix of partial derivatives. The chain rule becomes matrix multiplication of Jacobians. This structure underlies backpropagation in neural networks.

Applications: Optimization

Finding maxima and minima of multivariable functions requires setting all partial derivatives to zero—a critical point satisfies grad(f) = 0. The second derivative test uses the Hessian matrix H (matrix of second partial derivatives) to classify: if H is positive definite, the point is a local minimum; negative definite means a local maximum; indefinite means a saddle point. Constrained optimization with equality constraints g(x,y) = 0 uses Lagrange multipliers.

Conclusion

The gradient extends the fundamental idea of a derivative—instantaneous rate of change—from one dimension to many. It captures not just how fast a function changes but in which direction, pointing toward optima and encoding the geometry of surfaces. Whether you're training a neural network via gradient descent, calculating electric field from potential energy, or finding the most efficient production plan, the gradient is the mathematical compass that reveals the direction of greatest change.
