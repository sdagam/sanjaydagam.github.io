Confidence Intervals: What Polls Really Tell Us

August 17, 2024

Before every election, pollsters survey a few thousand people and report results like '52% support Candidate A, with a margin of error of plus or minus 3 percentage points.' This margin of error is a confidence interval—a range of values likely to contain the true population parameter. Confidence intervals are among statistics' most useful and most misunderstood concepts, appearing in medical research, quality control, scientific measurements, and public opinion polling.

Why We Need Intervals

A single survey of 1,000 people cannot exactly determine the opinion of 300 million. Different random samples give different results. A point estimate (52%) gives no sense of this uncertainty. A confidence interval quantifies it: rather than claiming the true proportion is exactly 52%, we claim it's likely between 49% and 55%. The interval honestly represents what the data can and cannot tell us.

Constructing a Confidence Interval

For a proportion p estimated from n observations, the 95% confidence interval is approximately: p_hat +/- 1.96 * sqrt(p_hat*(1-p_hat)/n). The 1.96 comes from the standard normal distribution—95% of a normal distribution falls within 1.96 standard deviations of the mean. For a poll of 1,000 people with 52% supporting a candidate: SE = sqrt(0.52*0.48/1000) = 0.0158, giving interval 52% +/- 3.1%.

The Correct Interpretation

The most common misinterpretation: 'There is a 95% probability that the true proportion falls in this interval.' This is wrong. The correct interpretation: 'This interval was constructed using a procedure that captures the true parameter 95% of the time.' Imagining 100 different polls conducted with the same methodology, about 95 of the resulting intervals would contain the true proportion.

Factors Affecting Width

Confidence interval width depends on three factors. Sample size: larger samples give narrower intervals. The margin of error shrinks as 1/sqrt(n)—to halve the margin of error, you need four times as many observations. Confidence level: a 99% interval is wider than a 95% interval. Variability: more variable populations require wider intervals.

Beyond Proportions

Confidence intervals apply to any estimated parameter. For a mean estimated from n observations: CI = x_bar +/- t*(s/sqrt(n)), where t comes from the t-distribution. Medical studies report confidence intervals for treatment effects—a drug might reduce blood pressure by 5 mmHg with a 95% CI of [2, 8] mmHg. If the interval excludes zero, the effect is statistically significant.

Conclusion

Confidence intervals communicate what data can tell us, with appropriate humility about what remains uncertain. They transform a single estimate into a range that honestly reflects sampling variability. Understanding them requires resisting the temptation to interpret the confidence level as a probability about a specific interval—it's a statement about the reliability of the process that produced it.
