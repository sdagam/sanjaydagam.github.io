Dimensional Analysis: How Units Unlock Physics Problems

November 16, 2024

How does the period of a pendulum depend on its length, mass, and the strength of gravity? You could derive it from Newton's laws with careful mathematics. Or you could figure out most of the answer in two minutes using dimensional analysis—without solving a single equation. Dimensional analysis is the technique of using the physical dimensions of quantities to constrain the form of physical relationships. It can derive formulas, check calculations, suggest experiments, and estimate the magnitudes of quantities in unfamiliar domains.

Dimensions and Units

Every physical quantity has dimensions—combinations of base dimensions like Length [L], Mass [M], Time [T], Temperature [Theta], and Electric Current [I]. Velocity has dimensions [L][T^-1]; force has dimensions [M][L][T^-2]. Equations must be dimensionally consistent—you cannot add a velocity to a force, and both sides of an equation must have the same dimensions. This consistency requirement is both a check on calculations and a tool for deriving formulas.

The Pendulum Example

A pendulum's period T might depend on its length L, mass m, and gravitational acceleration g (dimensions [L][T^-2]). We seek T = C * L^a * m^b * g^c. Matching dimensions gives: c = -1/2, a = 1/2, b = 0. Therefore T = C * sqrt(L/g). Dimensional analysis tells us the period is proportional to sqrt(L/g) and independent of mass—without any physics calculation!

The Buckingham Pi Theorem

The Buckingham Pi theorem formalizes dimensional analysis. If a physical equation involves n variables with k independent dimensions, the equation can be written in terms of n - k dimensionless groups (Pi groups). For the pendulum: 3 variables, 2 dimensions, so 1 dimensionless group: Pi = T*sqrt(g/L) = constant. This gives T = constant * sqrt(L/g).

Estimates and Fermi Problems

Dimensional analysis enables order-of-magnitude estimates of unfamiliar quantities. The energy of a nuclear explosion: nuclear energy density is about 10^13 joules per kilogram. A bomb contains perhaps 10 kg of active material, giving ~10^14 joules—a reasonable estimate for Hiroshima-scale bombs. Dimensional analysis doesn't give exact answers but often gives correct order of magnitude—invaluable for sanity-checking calculations.

Applications in Fluid Mechanics

Dimensional analysis is particularly powerful in fluid mechanics, where complex experiments can be guided by dimensionless numbers. The Reynolds number Re = rho*v*L/mu determines whether flow is laminar or turbulent. The Mach number Ma = v/c characterizes compressibility effects. These dimensionless groups mean that experiments on scale models give results applicable to full-scale systems.

Conclusion

Dimensional analysis converts physical intuition about how quantities relate into mathematical constraints on the forms of equations. It cannot give you the numerical constants in physical laws—those require theory or experiment—but it can tell you everything else: how one variable depends on others, which variables matter, and what dimensionless combinations to plot experimentally.
