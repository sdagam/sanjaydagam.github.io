Correlation vs. Causation: Why the Distinction Matters

September 21, 2024

Ice cream sales and drowning rates both peak in summer. Countries with more television sets per capita have higher life expectancies. People who carry lighters are more likely to get lung cancer (even non-smokers). In each case, two variables move together—they're correlated—but nobody would claim that ice cream causes drownings. The distinction between correlation and causation is one of statistics' most important lessons and one of humanity's most persistent cognitive errors.

What Correlation Measures

Pearson's correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables. It ranges from -1 (perfect negative linear relationship) through 0 (no linear relationship) to +1 (perfect positive linear relationship). Importantly, correlation only measures linear relationships—two variables can be perfectly related in a nonlinear way and have r = 0.

Why Correlation Doesn't Imply Causation

Three structures produce correlation without causation. Common cause (confounding): both variables are caused by a third variable. Ice cream sales and drowning rates are both caused by hot weather. Reverse causation: the effect is mistaken for the cause. Chance: with enough variables, some will correlate by random chance. The website tylervigen.com catalogs spurious correlations: per capita cheese consumption correlates with deaths by bedsheet tangling.

Establishing Causation

Causation requires more than correlation. The gold standard is the Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT): randomly assign subjects to treatment and control groups. Random assignment ensures the groups are statistically identical in all relevant variables—observed and unobserved—before treatment. Any difference in outcomes can then be attributed to the treatment.

Causal Inference Methods

Several statistical methods attempt to estimate causal effects from observational data. Instrumental variables use a variable that affects treatment but not the outcome directly. Regression discontinuity exploits sharp cutoffs. Difference-in-differences compares changes over time between treated and untreated groups. Each method requires assumptions that must be carefully justified.

Why It Matters So Much

Confusing correlation for causation leads to bad decisions across every domain. Public health: early studies found coffee consumption correlated with heart disease—but coffee drinkers also smoked more. Controlling for smoking eliminated most of the association. Technology: recommendation algorithms optimize for engagement, finding correlations between content and clicks—but maximizing correlations may cause harm that shows up elsewhere.

Conclusion

Correlation is a mathematically precise measure of association; causation is a claim about mechanism and intervention. Separating them requires experimental design or clever observational strategies. In a world flooded with data and correlations, the question 'but does it cause it?' is among the most important you can ask—and answering it rigorously requires understanding why the distinction matters and what it takes to overcome it.
