Terminal Velocity: When Falling Objects Stop Accelerating

January 20, 2024

Drop a feather and a bowling ball from the same height, and they land at different times—but not for the reason Aristotle thought. Aristotle believed heavier objects fall faster by nature. Galileo proved otherwise with his famous Leaning Tower of Pisa experiment. The truth involves a beautiful interplay between gravity, air resistance, and a fundamental concept in differential equations: terminal velocity. Understanding it reveals how mathematical modeling captures physical reality.

The Forces at Play

When an object falls through air, two forces act on it: gravity pulls it down, and air resistance (drag) pushes it up. Gravity is constant: F_gravity = mg, where m is mass and g is gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s^2 on Earth). Drag is not constant—it increases with velocity. For most objects at everyday speeds, drag follows F_drag = bv, where b is a drag coefficient depending on shape, size, and air density, and v is velocity. For faster objects (like skydivers), drag scales as bv^2.

The Differential Equation

Newton's second law gives us: m(dv/dt) = mg - bv. This is a first-order linear differential equation. The solution is: v(t) = (mg/b)(1 - e^(-bt/m)). This describes velocity starting at 0 and approaching mg/b as t approaches infinity. That limiting value is terminal velocity: v_terminal = mg/b. The exponential decay means the object approaches terminal velocity asymptotically—never quite reaching it mathematically, but effectively achieving it in finite time.

Why Mass Matters

Terminal velocity equals mg/b, so heavier objects (larger m) have higher terminal velocities. A bowling ball is denser and heavier than a feather, so it reaches a much higher terminal velocity before drag balances gravity. In a vacuum—where b = 0—both objects accelerate indefinitely at the same rate, which is why Galileo's vacuum experiment would show equal fall times regardless of mass. On the Moon, with negligible atmosphere, astronaut David Scott famously dropped a hammer and feather simultaneously in 1971—they landed together.

Real-World Applications

Understanding terminal velocity is crucial in many fields. Skydivers reach about 55 m/s (195 km/h) in a spread-eagle position, or 90 m/s (320 km/h) headfirst—and they survive because parachutes dramatically increase drag, lowering terminal velocity to safe landing speeds. Engineers design cars with aerodynamic shapes to minimize b, increasing fuel efficiency. Raindrops fall at terminal velocity—without air resistance, a raindrop falling from a cloud would hit the ground at hundreds of meters per second, turning rain into a lethal event.

Beyond Simple Models

Real drag is more complex than bv. At high speeds, drag scales as bv^2, giving terminal velocity proportional to sqrt(mg/b) instead. Objects can also rotate, creating lift forces (the Magnus effect), explaining why curveballs curve. The full Navier-Stokes equations governing fluid flow around objects are notoriously difficult to solve analytically, which is why aircraft designers rely heavily on wind tunnel testing and computational simulation.

Conclusion

Terminal velocity elegantly demonstrates how differential equations capture physical processes. The simple equation m(dv/dt) = mg - bv contains within it the entire story of falling: acceleration from rest, the growing resistance of air, and the asymptotic approach to a speed where forces balance. It shows how mathematics translates physical intuition—the sense that falling can't go on accelerating forever—into precise, predictive equations. The feather and bowling ball aren't just a physics demonstration; they're an invitation to see the differential equations governing everything that moves.
