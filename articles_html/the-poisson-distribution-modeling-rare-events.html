The Poisson Distribution: Modeling Rare Events

November 30, 2024

How many customers arrive at a bank in a given hour? How many typos appear per page of a manuscript? How many radioactive decays occur per second in a sample? These questions share a common structure: counting the number of times a rare event occurs in a fixed interval of time or space, where events happen independently and at a constant average rate. The Poisson distribution provides the probability model for exactly this situation.

Deriving the Distribution

The Poisson distribution emerges as the limit of the binomial distribution for rare events. As n approaches infinity with lambda = n*p fixed: P(X = k) = e^(-lambda) * lambda^k / k!, where lambda is the average number of events per interval. For small lambda, the distribution is strongly right-skewed. As lambda grows, it becomes more symmetric and approaches a normal distribution.

Properties

The Poisson distribution has elegant mathematical properties. The mean equals the variance: E[X] = Var[X] = lambda. This equal mean-variance property is a diagnostic: for count data, if the sample mean approximately equals the sample variance, Poisson is a reasonable model. The sum of independent Poisson variables is Poisson: if X ~ Poisson(lambda_1) and Y ~ Poisson(lambda_2), then X + Y ~ Poisson(lambda_1 + lambda_2).

The Poisson Process

A Poisson process with rate lambda satisfies: events occur one at a time, independently, at constant average rate lambda. The number of events in time [0,t] follows Poisson(lambda*t). The waiting time between successive events follows an exponential distribution with mean 1/lambda. The memoryless property of the exponential distribution reflects the Poisson process's time-homogeneity.

Applications

Poisson applications pervade science and industry. Queuing theory uses Poisson arrival rates to model customers at service facilities. Telecommunications engineers model call arrivals as Poisson processes. Insurance actuaries model claims as Poisson processes. Genomics uses Poisson models to detect regions of the genome with unusually many or few mutations. Quality control uses Poisson to model defect counts per unit of production.

When Poisson Fails

The Poisson distribution assumes events are independent and occur at a constant rate. Overdispersion (variance > mean) occurs when events cluster—disease cases cluster in households. The negative binomial distribution handles overdispersion. Time-varying rates require non-homogeneous Poisson processes where lambda(t) changes over time—modeling hospital admissions that peak during flu season or traffic that varies by time of day.

Conclusion

The Poisson distribution is nature's model for countable randomness—the distribution that governs how often rare, independent events happen in a fixed window of time or space. Its single parameter lambda simultaneously determines mean and variance. From cosmic rays to customer queues, from radioactive atoms to website clicks, the Poisson distribution captures the statistical signature of pure randomness in the world's discrete events.
