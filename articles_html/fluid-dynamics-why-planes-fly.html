Fluid Dynamics: Why Planes Fly

August 3, 2024

Every day, millions of people board aircraft weighing hundreds of tons and trust them to remain airborne. The force that makes this possible—lift—emerges from the interaction between a wing's shape and the air flowing around it. Fluid dynamics, the branch of physics governing how liquids and gases move, explains not just flight but also how ships navigate, how blood flows through arteries, how weather systems develop, and how engineers design everything from race cars to skyscrapers.

Bernoulli's Principle

The most common explanation of lift invokes Bernoulli's principle: in a flowing fluid, higher velocity corresponds to lower pressure. A wing (airfoil) is shaped so that air traveling over the curved upper surface moves faster than air traveling under the flatter lower surface. By Bernoulli's principle, faster air above means lower pressure above, while slower air below means higher pressure below. This pressure difference pushes the wing upward—lift. Bernoulli's equation: P + (1/2)*rho*v^2 + rho*g*h = constant.

The Full Picture: Circulation and Angle of Attack

Bernoulli's principle alone is incomplete. A more complete explanation involves circulation—the net rotation of air around the wing—and angle of attack. A wing tilted slightly upward deflects air downward; by Newton's third law, the air pushes the wing upward. The Kutta-Joukowski theorem relates lift per unit span to circulation: L = rho * v * Gamma. Both effects contribute: wing shape creates some circulation, and angle of attack creates more.

The Navier-Stokes Equations

The complete mathematical description of fluid flow is given by the Navier-Stokes equations: rho(du/dt + u*grad(u)) = -grad(P) + mu*nabla^2(u) + rho*g. Whether smooth solutions always exist for three-dimensional flows is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems in mathematics, with a $1 million prize. In practice, engineers solve approximate discretized versions using computational fluid dynamics (CFD).

Turbulence

At low speeds, fluid flows in smooth, orderly layers—laminar flow. Above a critical speed characterized by the Reynolds number Re = rho*v*L/mu, flow becomes turbulent—chaotic, mixing, with swirling eddies at all scales. Turbulence dramatically increases drag and changes lift characteristics. Turbulence modeling—approximating the effects of small-scale eddies—is a major research area in engineering.

Applications Beyond Flight

Fluid dynamics shapes countless technologies. Formula 1 cars use downforce from inverted wings to stay on the track at high speeds. Ship hulls are designed to minimize wave drag. Wind turbines extract energy from fluid flow. The human cardiovascular system is a fluid dynamics problem. Meteorologists simulate fluid dynamics in the atmosphere to forecast weather.

Conclusion

Flight is fluid dynamics made tangible—the abstract mathematics of pressure, velocity, and circulation expressed as a 400-ton aircraft defying gravity. The Navier-Stokes equations that govern it are simultaneously completely known and largely unsolvable, capturing how much of physics works: the governing laws are clear, but their consequences are extraordinarily rich and complex.
