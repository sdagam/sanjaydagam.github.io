Related Rates: How Fast Is That Shadow Growing?

March 2, 2024

A streetlight stands 20 feet tall. A person 6 feet tall walks away from it at 5 feet per second. How fast is the person's shadow growing? This is a related rates problem—one of calculus's most practical applications. Related rates problems ask: given that some quantity is changing at a known rate, how fast is a related quantity changing? They appear in physics, engineering, economics, and everyday situations, and solving them requires combining geometric relationships with differentiation through the chain rule.

The Method

Related rates problems follow a consistent procedure. First, draw a diagram and identify all variables. Second, write an equation relating the variables—often from geometry or physics. Third, differentiate both sides with respect to time, using the chain rule for every variable that changes with time. Fourth, substitute known values and solve for the unknown rate. The key insight is that every changing quantity is implicitly a function of time, so every variable in your equation can be differentiated with respect to t.

The Shadow Problem Solved

Let x be the distance from the person to the streetlight, and s be the length of the shadow. By similar triangles: 20/(x + s) = 6/s. Cross-multiplying: 20s = 6(x + s) = 6x + 6s, so 14s = 6x, giving s = (3/7)x. Now differentiate with respect to time: ds/dt = (3/7)(dx/dt). We know dx/dt = 5 ft/s (the person's walking speed), so ds/dt = (3/7)(5) = 15/7 ≈ 2.14 ft/s. The shadow grows at about 2.14 feet per second—regardless of how far the person has walked. The linear relationship between shadow length and distance means the shadow grows at a constant rate.

The Balloon Problem

A classic related rates problem: a spherical balloon is being inflated at 100 cubic centimeters per second. How fast is the radius growing when the radius is 5 cm? The relationship: V = (4/3)pi*r^3. Differentiating: dV/dt = 4*pi*r^2 * dr/dt. We know dV/dt = 100 cm^3/s and r = 5 cm, so: 100 = 4*pi*25 * dr/dt, giving dr/dt = 100/(100*pi) = 1/pi ≈ 0.318 cm/s. Notice that as the balloon grows, dr/dt decreases—the same volume added per second produces a smaller radius change when the balloon is larger, because the surface area (4*pi*r^2) is greater.

The Ladder Problem

A 10-foot ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at 2 ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 6 feet from the wall? By the Pythagorean theorem: x^2 + y^2 = 100, where x is the distance from the wall and y is the height. Differentiating: 2x(dx/dt) + 2y(dy/dt) = 0. When x = 6: y = sqrt(100 - 36) = 8. So: 2(6)(2) + 2(8)(dy/dt) = 0, giving dy/dt = -24/16 = -1.5 ft/s. The negative sign confirms the top is moving downward. Notice the speed increases as the ladder becomes more horizontal.

Applications Beyond Textbooks

Related rates appear constantly in applied contexts. In medicine, clinicians use related rates to understand how drug concentration changes as it's absorbed and metabolized. Engineers use them to calculate how quickly a cone-shaped pile of sand grows as material is added. Economists use them to analyze how quickly market prices adjust to supply changes. In fluid dynamics, the continuity equation is a related rates equation connecting fluid velocity to cross-sectional area. Any situation where multiple changing quantities are geometrically or physically linked requires related rates reasoning.

Conclusion

Related rates problems are calculus in its most applied form—transforming geometric or physical relationships into statements about rates of change. The chain rule is the key that connects rates of different quantities, and the skill lies in correctly identifying and differentiating the relationship between variables. From the shadow growing behind you on a sunny day to the changing pressure in a deflating tire, related rates are the mathematical language for describing how the world's interconnected quantities evolve together through time.
