Control Theory: How Thermostats and Autopilots Work

August 10, 2024

Your home thermostat measures temperature, compares it to your desired setting, and turns heating or cooling on or off to reduce the difference. This simple feedback loop—measure, compare, correct—is the essence of control theory. The same mathematical framework that governs your thermostat also controls autopilots keeping aircraft on course, industrial robots assembling cars, rockets adjusting their trajectory, and insulin pumps managing diabetics' blood sugar. Control theory is the engineering science of making systems behave as desired despite disturbances and uncertainty.

Open vs. Closed Loop Control

Open-loop control applies a fixed input without measuring the output. A toaster set for 2 minutes applies heat for exactly that time regardless of whether the bread is toasted. Closed-loop control (feedback control) measures the actual output and adjusts the input based on the difference between desired and actual output. Closed-loop systems are more robust to disturbances but introduce the possibility of instability.

The PID Controller

The most widely used control algorithm is the PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller. Given an error e(t) = desired - actual, the control signal is: u(t) = K_p*e(t) + K_i*integral(e dt) + K_d*(de/dt). The proportional term responds to current error. The integral term accumulates past errors—it eliminates steady-state offset. The derivative term anticipates future error—it dampens oscillations.

Stability

A control system is stable if its output remains bounded for bounded inputs. Stability analysis uses the Laplace transform to convert differential equations into algebraic ones, enabling analysis in the frequency domain. The Routh-Hurwitz criterion, Nyquist plots, and Bode diagrams are classical tools for assessing stability. The gain margin and phase margin quantify how close a system is to instability.

Modern Control: State Space

Modern control theory uses state-space representation: dx/dt = Ax + Bu, y = Cx + Du. This formulation handles multi-input, multi-output systems elegantly and connects control theory to linear algebra. The Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) finds the optimal control law that minimizes a cost function balancing control effort and deviation from desired state—the mathematics of autopilots and spacecraft attitude control.

Autopilots and Robotics

Aircraft autopilots are sophisticated multi-loop control systems. An inner loop controls attitude (pitch, roll, yaw) using control surfaces. An outer loop controls trajectory by commanding attitude changes. Modern fly-by-wire aircraft have no mechanical connection between pilot input and control surfaces—computers interpret pilot commands and send signals to actuators, with control laws designed to make the aircraft feel natural.

Conclusion

Control theory transforms the intuitive idea of feedback—adjusting your actions based on results—into a precise mathematical framework. The PID controller, despite its simplicity, keeps aircraft stable, industrial processes consistent, and home temperatures comfortable. More sophisticated methods optimize performance and handle uncertainty. In a world of sensors and actuators, control theory is the mathematics that closes the loop between measurement and action.
