The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: Bridging Derivatives and Integrals

December 7, 2024

Differentiation and integration appear at first to be opposite operations—one finds instantaneous rates of change, the other accumulates quantities over intervals. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) reveals they are, in a precise mathematical sense, inverse operations. This theorem is one of mathematics' most profound results: connecting two independently motivated problems through a deep structural relationship.

Part 1: The Accumulation Function

Define F(x) = integral from a to x of f(t) dt—the area under f from a to x as a function of the upper limit x. The FTC Part 1 states: if f is continuous on [a,b], then F is differentiable and F'(x) = f(x). The derivative of the accumulation function equals the original function. Intuitively: the rate at which area accumulates at position x equals the height of f at x.

Part 2: The Evaluation Theorem

If F is any antiderivative of f (meaning F'(x) = f(x)), then: integral from a to b of f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a). This is the result that makes integration computable without summing infinitely many rectangles. To find the area under a curve, find an antiderivative and evaluate it at the endpoints. For example: integral from 0 to 1 of x^2 dx = [x^3/3] from 0 to 1 = 1/3.

Why It's Profound

Before the FTC, differentiation and integration were separate mathematical fields. The FTC revealed they are inverses of each other—just as multiplication and division are inverses. This unification, achieved independently by Newton and Leibniz in the 17th century, created calculus as we know it. It also made integration practical: instead of computing limits of Riemann sums, we only need antiderivatives.

The Net Change Theorem

A direct consequence of Part 2 is the net change theorem: integral from a to b of f'(x) dx = f(b) - f(a). The integral of a rate of change equals the net change. If v(t) is velocity: integral of v(t) dt = net displacement. If a force F(x) acts over a distance: integral of F dx = work done = change in kinetic energy. The FTC connects the microscopic to the macroscopic across physics, biology, and engineering.

Improper Integrals and Extensions

The FTC extends to improper integrals—those with infinite limits or unbounded integrands. Integral from 1 to infinity of 1/x^2 dx = [-1/x] from 1 to infinity = 1. The p-test states the integral from 1 to infinity of 1/x^p dx converges if and only if p > 1. These improper integrals appear throughout probability and physics.

Conclusion

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the keystone of the entire edifice of calculus. By revealing that differentiation and integration are inverse operations, it unifies two seemingly separate mathematical fields and provides the practical tool that makes calculus applicable to the real world. Every time you compute an area, evaluate a probability, or find the total change in any quantity from its rate of change, the Fundamental Theorem is at work.
