Regression to the Mean: Why Extremes Don't Last

June 29, 2024

In 1886, Francis Galton measured the heights of parents and their adult children. He noticed something puzzling: unusually tall parents tended to have children shorter than themselves, and unusually short parents tended to have children taller than themselves. Children's heights regressed toward the average. Galton called this 'regression to mediocrity'—now known as regression to the mean. This statistical phenomenon is one of the most misunderstood in science, responsible for countless false conclusions about the effects of interventions.

Why It Happens

Any measured outcome combines true underlying ability or value with random variation. An exceptional score reflects both genuine extremeness and favorable random variation. When measured again, the random component is new and independent, while the true component remains roughly the same. The second measurement's extreme component is likely to be less extreme.

The Galton Board

Galton illustrated this with his famous quincunx (Galton board)—a pegboard where balls fall through rows of pins, spreading into a normal distribution at the bottom. Extreme positions result from all-rightward sequences, which are rarer than sequences mixing left and right. The next step is most likely toward center—not because of any corrective force, but because that's where most paths lead.

The Danger of False Causation

Regression to the mean produces apparent treatment effects where none exist. Students who perform poorly on a test receive tutoring and improve on the next test. Did tutoring help? Maybe—but some improvement would happen anyway through regression to the mean. Without a control group, you can't separate regression effects from genuine improvement. This confound affects medical studies, business interventions, and educational research constantly.

Sports and the Sophomore Slump

Sports provide vivid examples. A rookie baseball player with an extraordinary first season often has a worse second season—the 'sophomore slump.' Exceptional first seasons reflect both genuine talent and statistical luck. The player's true ability didn't change; the luck component reverted toward normal. Similarly, the Sports Illustrated cover jinx—athletes featured on the cover often perform worse afterward—is partly regression to the mean.

Correct Interpretation

Recognizing regression to the mean requires always asking: was this group or individual selected based on extreme performance? If yes, expect regression. The cure for misinterpretation is randomized controlled trials: randomly assign people to treatment and control groups before measuring outcomes. Both groups regress to the mean equally, so any remaining difference represents the genuine treatment effect.

Conclusion

Regression to the mean is not a force pushing outcomes toward average—it's a mathematical consequence of measurement variation combined with selection of extremes. Galton's observation about heights launched statistics as a discipline, giving us correlation and regression analysis. Understanding this phenomenon prevents false conclusions about interventions and explains why exceptional outcomes, selected precisely because they're exceptional, tend not to last.
