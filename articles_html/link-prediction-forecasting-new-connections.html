Link Prediction: Forecasting New Connections

October 5, 2024

LinkedIn suggests people you might know. Netflix predicts which movies you'll enjoy. Amazon recommends products frequently bought together. Behind all these recommendations lies a mathematical problem: given a network observed at one point in time, predict which new connections will form in the future. Link prediction is the task of inferring missing or future edges in a network, and it sits at the intersection of graph theory, machine learning, and applied statistics.

Why Networks Have Missing Links

Network data is never complete. Social network surveys miss connections people don't report. Protein interaction networks are limited by the experiments conducted. Citation networks are observed at a point in time; future citations are unknown but partly predictable. The link prediction problem addresses all these cases: given an incomplete or historical snapshot of a network, assign a score to each non-edge indicating how likely it is to be a true or future edge.

Similarity-Based Methods

The simplest link prediction methods score non-edges based on the similarity of the two nodes. Common neighbors: two nodes are likely connected if they share many mutual friends. The Jaccard coefficient normalizes this: J(u,v) = |N(u) intersection N(v)| / |N(u) union N(v)|. The Adamic-Adar index weights common neighbors by the inverse log of their degree. Preferential attachment scores the product of degrees, reflecting the rich-get-richer dynamic.

Path-Based Methods

The Katz index sums over all paths between two nodes, weighting shorter paths more heavily: Katz(u,v) = sum over l of beta^l * (number of paths of length l). For small beta, this approximately equals the number of length-2 paths (common neighbors). The matrix formulation: Katz scores are given by (I - beta*A)^(-1) - I, where A is the adjacency matrix.

Matrix Factorization and Embeddings

Modern link prediction uses machine learning. Matrix factorization decomposes the adjacency matrix A into low-rank factors: A approximately equals U*V^T. The dot product of two rows gives the predicted connection probability. Node embedding methods like node2vec learn vector representations of nodes such that connected nodes have similar vectors; link probability is then estimated from vector similarity.

Evaluation

Link prediction algorithms are evaluated by hiding a fraction of known edges, running the algorithm on the remaining network, and measuring how well it recovers the hidden edges. Standard metrics include the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) and Average Precision. A random predictor achieves AUC = 0.5; good methods achieve 0.9+.

Conclusion

Link prediction transforms the observation that networks have structure into actionable predictions about future or missing connections. From simple neighbor-counting heuristics to sophisticated embedding methods, the field has developed powerful tools for inferring what a network will look like tomorrow based on what it looks like today. Link prediction is the mathematical engine behind the recommendations that increasingly shape our digital lives.
