Bipartite Networks: Modeling Two-Mode Systems

December 14, 2024

Most network models connect nodes of one type—people connected to people, websites connected to websites. But many real systems involve two distinct types of entities with connections only between types, not within them. Actors connect to movies. Customers connect to products. Genes connect to diseases. These two-mode systems are bipartite networks, and they require specialized mathematical treatment that reveals structure invisible in single-mode analysis.

Structure of Bipartite Graphs

A bipartite graph G = (U, V, E) has two sets of nodes U and V with edges only between U and V, never within U or within V. The biadjacency matrix B has rows indexed by U and columns by V, with B_ij = 1 if node i in U is connected to node j in V. Every bipartite graph has the property that it contains no odd-length cycles. All eigenvalues of a bipartite graph's adjacency matrix come in +/- pairs.

One-Mode Projections

Bipartite networks are often analyzed through their one-mode projections. The U-projection connects two U-nodes if they share at least one V-neighbor: P_U = B*B^T. For an actor-movie network, the U-projection connects actors who appeared in the same film. The V-projection connects movies sharing cast members. Weighted projections preserve this information but require careful normalization to avoid bias toward high-degree nodes.

The Degree Distribution

Bipartite networks have two degree distributions—one for each node set. The relationship between degree distributions in the two modes is constrained: the sum of degrees in U equals the sum of degrees in V (both equal the number of edges). This constraint links the two distributions mathematically.

Nestedness and Modularity

Two important structural properties characterize bipartite networks. Nestedness describes a hierarchical ordering: specialists interact with a subset of what generalists interact with. Highly nested ecological networks are thought to be more stable. Modularity in bipartite networks identifies groups of U-nodes that interact preferentially with specific groups of V-nodes—discovering product categories from purchase data or disease modules from gene-disease associations.

Applications: Recommendation Systems

Bipartite networks are the natural model for collaborative filtering in recommendation systems. The user-item network has users connected to items they've rated or purchased. Matrix factorization of the biadjacency matrix B decomposes it into latent factors: B approximately equals U*V^T. Netflix's recommendation system, Amazon's product recommendations, and Spotify's music discovery all implement variants of this bipartite matrix factorization.

Conclusion

Bipartite networks provide the natural mathematical framework for two-mode systems—the ubiquitous situations where connections bridge distinct categories rather than linking similar ones. From recommendation algorithms to ecological analyses to economic analyses of country-product complexity, bipartite network analysis extracts insight from the structure of between-type connections.
