Network Science · Epidemiology

Safe Zones: The Paradigm Effect in Epidemic Intervention

Investigating a novel phenomenon in epidemic dynamics on complex networks: the "paradigm effect" where betweenness centrality-based edge removal outperforms random removal during active epidemics but underperforms at equilibrium states. This research explores the counterintuitive Bridge Preservation Paradox—a phase transition where random removal strategies become more effective at high intervention intensities on scale-free networks.

Using synthetic network topologies including Barabási-Albert and Erdős-Rényi models, combined with SIS/SIR epidemic simulations, this work challenges conventional assumptions about optimal intervention strategies in disease spread.

Atmospheric Science

Wisconsin Ozone Dynamics and Lake Breeze Effects

Investigating temporal and spatial gradients in ozone concentrations across Wisconsin, with particular focus on the unique meteorological effects created by Lake Michigan. This research examines how lake breeze circulation patterns influence air quality and pollutant transport in the region.

Analysis includes comprehensive climatology studies of precipitation patterns in Milwaukee, revealing concentration into heavier events and implications for regional climate trends.

Public Health · Statistics

LGBTQ+ Youth Mental Health Disparities in Wisconsin

Applying sophisticated causal inference methodologies to analyze mental health disparities among LGBTQ+ youth across Wisconsin counties. This research employs mediation analysis and propensity score techniques to identify contributing factors and potential interventions.

The goal is to generate evidence-based insights that can inform policy and support programs targeting vulnerable youth populations.

Network Science

Synthetic vs. Real-World Network Validation

Comparative analysis of epidemic spreading dynamics on synthetic network models versus real-world contact networks. This work explores the validity of using simplified models like Barabási-Albert and Erdős-Rényi networks as proxies for actual human interaction patterns in epidemiological modeling.
