Network Science Epidemiology
Project 01

A Study in Creation of Safe Zones Using Link Removal in Epidemiology

Supervisor: Prof. Laura Schaposnik, University of Illinois Chicago

Plain-English Summary

Imagine trying to stop a disease from spreading by cutting off connections between people — like closing certain roads to prevent traffic. This project asks: which connections should you cut, and when? We discovered a surprising paradox: the "smartest" targeting strategy actually backfires at high intervention levels, making random removal the better choice. We call this the Bridge Preservation Paradox.

Abstract

This study investigates a novel phenomenon in epidemic dynamics on complex networks — the "paradigm effect" — where betweenness centrality-based edge removal outperforms random removal during active epidemics but underperforms at equilibrium. We characterize a phase transition in intervention efficacy as a function of removal intensity on scale-free networks.

Methods

SIS and SIR epidemic simulations on Barabási-Albert and Erdős-Rényi synthetic network topologies. Edge removal strategies evaluated: betweenness centrality, random, degree-based, and hybrid. Monte Carlo averaging over 500+ network realizations per parameter set.

Key Findings

  • Betweenness-based removal is superior at low intervention intensities
  • A phase transition exists beyond which random removal dominates
  • Scale-free networks are uniquely susceptible to the Bridge Preservation Paradox
  • Network topology — not just epidemic parameters — determines optimal strategy

Real-World Implications

Public health agencies cannot rely on a single "best" contact-tracing strategy. Intervention intensity must be calibrated to network structure — over-targeting high-centrality contacts can paradoxically accelerate spread during active outbreaks.

Project 02

Network Structure Determines the Mechanism of Targeted Epidemic Containment

Supervisor: Prof. Laura Schaposnik, University of Illinois Chicago

Plain-English Summary

Building on the safe zones project, this work asks a deeper question: can we mathematically prove why certain containment strategies work — not just observe it in simulations? We develop formal analytical proofs and validate them computationally across both idealized mathematical networks and real-world contact networks drawn from human interaction data.

Abstract

We present analytical proofs establishing that the dominant mechanism of targeted epidemic containment is determined by the underlying network's degree distribution and clustering coefficient. Results are validated computationally on both synthetic (Barabási-Albert, Watts-Strogatz) and empirical real-world contact networks.

Methods

Spectral graph theory, mean-field epidemic approximations, and rigorous mathematical proof construction. Computational validation using Python (NetworkX, NumPy) on real-world network datasets including hospital contact networks and social interaction graphs.

Key Findings

  • Analytical proofs confirm simulation-derived results from Project 01
  • Real-world networks exhibit containment behavior consistent with synthetic models
  • Degree heterogeneity is the primary structural determinant of strategy efficacy

Real-World Implications

Provides a mathematical framework health agencies can use to select containment strategies based on measurable network properties — moving from trial-and-error to principled, provably optimal intervention design.

Project 03

Examination of Racial Disparity in Math Performance Among Wisconsin Students

Self-directed

Plain-English Summary

Standardized test scores in Wisconsin show a persistent and troubling gap in math performance along racial lines. This project digs into the data to understand why that gap exists — separating the effects of school funding, teacher quality, and socioeconomic status to identify which levers actually move outcomes for students of color.

Abstract

Using Wisconsin DPI assessment data disaggregated by race and district, this study applies regression-based decomposition and causal inference methods to quantify the contributions of school-level, district-level, and socioeconomic factors to observed racial math achievement gaps.

Methods

Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition, multilevel regression modeling, and propensity score matching applied to Wisconsin DPI public datasets. Analysis conducted in R with robustness checks across multiple model specifications.

Key Findings

  • Significant unexplained gap remains after controlling for SES and funding
  • District-level resource allocation is a statistically significant predictor
  • Gaps widen in districts with higher student-to-counselor ratios

Real-World Implications

Findings can directly inform targeted resource allocation decisions by Wisconsin school districts and the Department of Public Instruction, identifying specific intervention points most likely to reduce disparities.

Project 04

Impact of Rurality and Political Voting on LGBTQ Suicide Risk in Wisconsin

Self-directed

Plain-English Summary

LGBTQ youth in rural areas face dramatically higher mental health risks than their urban peers — but is it rurality itself, or the political environment that often comes with it? This project uses county-level Wisconsin data to untangle these two factors, asking whether a community's political climate independently predicts suicide risk among LGBTQ youth, beyond geography alone.

Abstract

This study applies mediation analysis and propensity score techniques to Wisconsin county-level health and voting data to isolate the independent effects of rurality and political environment on LGBTQ youth suicide risk, controlling for access to mental health services, income, and demographics.

Methods

Mediation analysis (Baron-Kenny framework), inverse probability weighting, and negative binomial regression on Wisconsin BRFSS and county health rankings data. Political environment operationalized using county-level presidential voting margins.

Key Findings

  • Political environment mediates a significant portion of the rurality effect
  • Counties with restrictive political climates show elevated risk independent of rurality
  • Access to affirming mental health services is the strongest protective factor identified

Real-World Implications

Highlights the importance of placing affirming mental health resources in politically conservative rural counties — not just urban centers — to protect the most vulnerable LGBTQ youth populations in Wisconsin.

Project 05

Predictive Model of Wisconsin Precipitation (Inland and Lakeshore)

Supervisor: Paul Buchmann, Wisconsin DNR State Climatology Office

Plain-English Summary

Wisconsin's weather near Lake Michigan behaves very differently from its interior — lake-effect snowfall, onshore fog, and altered storm tracks make the lakeshore a unique climate zone. This project builds a predictive model that accounts for these differences, helping the state better forecast precipitation for flood management, agriculture, and emergency planning.

Abstract

We develop and validate a spatially stratified precipitation model for Wisconsin that separately parameterizes inland and lakeshore climate zones, capturing lake breeze circulation effects and orographic influences using historical NOAA station data and ERA5 reanalysis products.

Methods

Time-series decomposition, ARIMA and gradient-boosted regression tree models, spatial interpolation (kriging), and cross-validation against held-out NOAA weather station records. ERA5 reanalysis data used for atmospheric covariates.

Key Findings

  • Lakeshore and inland zones require distinct model architectures
  • Lake breeze indices improve predictive accuracy by ~12% in lakeshore counties
  • Precipitation is concentrating into fewer, heavier events over time

Real-World Implications

More accurate precipitation forecasting directly supports Wisconsin DNR's flood planning, agricultural water management, and infrastructure resilience efforts across both lakeshore and inland communities.

Project 06

Study of Ozone Exceedance Days in Wisconsin Due to Canadian Wildfires

Supervisor: Ben Wolf, Director of Air Quality, Wisconsin DNR

Plain-English Summary

When Canadian wildfires send smoke drifting south into Wisconsin, ground-level ozone spikes — sometimes exceeding federal safety limits. This project quantifies how much of Wisconsin's air quality violations are caused by wildfire smoke versus local emissions, and models how this is changing as wildfires grow more frequent and severe with climate change.

Abstract

Using EPA AQS monitoring data and NOAA HYSPLIT back-trajectory analysis, we isolate and quantify the contribution of Canadian wildfire smoke transport to ozone exceedance days across Wisconsin monitoring stations, and construct a predictive attribution model linking fire weather indices to downstream air quality violations.

Methods

HYSPLIT atmospheric back-trajectory modeling, EPA AQS ozone time-series analysis, wildfire perimeter data from NIFC, and regression-based attribution modeling. Smoke contributions isolated using receptor modeling techniques.

Key Findings

  • Canadian wildfire smoke is a statistically significant driver of Wisconsin ozone exceedance days
  • Exceedance events cluster in summer months with northwesterly flow patterns
  • Wildfire contribution is increasing year-over-year consistent with rising fire activity

Real-World Implications

Supports Wisconsin DNR's case for "exceptional event" EPA designations, which can exclude wildfire-influenced days from compliance calculations — protecting Wisconsin from unfair air quality penalties for out-of-state emissions.

Project 07

Ground-Level NO₂ Predictions Using Satellite Imagery-Driven Convolutional Neural Networks

Supervisor: Prof. Tracey Holloway, University of Wisconsin–Madison

Plain-English Summary

Air quality monitors are expensive and sparse — most communities have no direct measurement of nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), a pollutant linked to asthma and heart disease. Satellites can observe the whole country, but measure the atmosphere from above, not at ground level. This project trains a neural network to bridge that gap: predicting ground-level NO₂ everywhere from satellite data alone.

Abstract

We develop a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on Sentinel-5P TROPOMI satellite NO₂ column retrievals, land use / land cover data, and meteorological covariates to predict surface-level NO₂ concentrations at 1 km resolution across the continental U.S., validated against EPA ground monitor observations.

Methods

Convolutional neural network architecture (PyTorch), Sentinel-5P TROPOMI satellite data, NLCD land use classification, MERRA-2 meteorological reanalysis, and EPA AQS ground-truth validation. Spatial cross-validation applied to prevent data leakage.

Key Findings

  • CNN outperforms traditional linear downscaling in urban areas
  • Land use context significantly improves satellite-to-ground translation
  • Model captures intraurban NO₂ gradients missed by sparse ground monitors

Real-World Implications

Enables high-resolution air quality mapping in communities without monitors — particularly low-income urban neighborhoods disproportionately exposed to traffic pollution — supporting environmental justice research and policy.

Agricultural Science Simulation Modeling
Project 08

UW–Madison Dairy Herd Structure Simulation and Cash Flow Tool

Supervisor: Prof. Victor Cabrera, UW–Madison

Plain-English Summary

Wisconsin dairy farmers make complex decisions every day — which cows to keep, which to sell, how to manage herd turnover — and the financial consequences can make or break a farm. This project builds a simulation tool that models herd dynamics and projects cash flow over time, giving farmers a practical way to stress-test decisions before making them in the real world.

Abstract

We develop a stochastic agent-based simulation of dairy herd dynamics incorporating lactation curves, culling decisions, reproduction rates, and milk price volatility. The model outputs projected cash flow distributions under user-specified management scenarios, designed for integration into UW–Madison's dairy decision-support platform.

Methods

Agent-based stochastic simulation (Python), Wood lactation curve parameterization, Monte Carlo cash flow projection, and sensitivity analysis across milk price, culling rate, and reproduction efficiency parameters. Validated against historical herd data from UW–Madison farms.

Key Findings

  • Culling rate is the dominant driver of cash flow variance in mid-size herds
  • Milk price volatility compounds herd-structure risk non-linearly
  • Simulation identifies optimal replacement timing windows under various price scenarios

Real-World Implications

Provides Wisconsin dairy farmers with an accessible decision-support tool to model the financial impact of herd management choices — supporting farm viability and long-term agricultural sustainability across the state.
