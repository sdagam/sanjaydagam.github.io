The Doppler Effect: Why Sirens Change Pitch

#13 March 30, 2024 Batch 1 · Jan–Jun 2024

An ambulance rushes toward you, siren wailing at a high pitch. It passes, and suddenly the pitch drops. Nothing about the siren changed—it emits the same frequency throughout. What changed is your relationship to the moving source. This phenomenon, the Doppler Effect, explains not just ambulance sirens but the expansion of the universe, speed radar guns, medical ultrasound, and weather forecasting.

The Physics of Wave Compression

When a source moves toward you, it partially catches up with the waves it emitted moments earlier. Each new wave crest is emitted from a position closer to the previous one, compressing the wavelength in front of the source. Compressed wavelength means higher frequency—higher pitch. Behind the moving source, the wavelength stretches, lowering the frequency.

The Mathematics

The observed frequency relates to the emitted frequency by:

f_observed = f_source × (v + v_observer) / (v + v_source)

where v is the wave speed. As v_s approaches v—the speed of sound—the denominator approaches zero and observed frequency approaches infinity. This is the sonic boom regime.

The Sonic Boom

When a source moves faster than the wave speed, it outruns its own waves. All compressed wave fronts pile up into a single shock wave—a cone of intense pressure. The angle of the Mach cone satisfies sin(θ) = v/v_s. Aircraft breaking the sound barrier create this cone continuously—the sonic boom isn't a one-time event but a persistent shock wave.

Radar, Ultrasound, and Cosmology

Police radar guns emit radio waves at a known frequency and measure the frequency of the reflection from your car—the Doppler shift reveals your speed. Medical ultrasound uses the Doppler Effect to measure blood flow velocity. In 1929, Edwin Hubble observed that light from distant galaxies is redshifted—shifted to lower frequencies—revealing that the universe is expanding.

Conclusion

The Doppler Effect demonstrates how motion fundamentally changes how we perceive waves. A single mathematical relationship—connecting emitted and observed frequency through relative velocities—unifies phenomena from traffic enforcement to cosmology. Whether detecting a tornado's rotation or measuring a galaxy's recession, the same principle applies: motion compresses or stretches waves, and that shift carries information.
